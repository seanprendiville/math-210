Section 6 Week 6

Lecture 6.1. Classes of continuous functions.

(a)

State and prove how continuity is preserved under addition, multiplication and taking reciprocals.
Solution.
Proposition 6.2. Algebra of continuity.

Let \(f, g : D \to \R\) with \(D \subset \R\text{.}\) Let \(x_0 \in D\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f\) and \(g\) are continuous at \(x_0\text{.}\) Then the sum \(f+g\) and product \(f\cdot g\) are both continuous at \(x_0\text{.}\)

If in addition \(g(x_0) \neq 0\text{,}\) then the quotient \(f/g\) is continuous at \(x_0\) (the quotient may not be defined on all of \(D\text{,}\) but it is defined on \(D \cap (x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)\) for some \(\delta \gt 0\text{,}\) see Proposition 5.20).

Proof.
We prove the result for the sum, the remaining arguments being similar (and left to the reader). By definition of function limits (Definition 5.11) and definition of continuity (Definition 5.13), we are required to show that if \((a_n)_n\) is a sequence in \(D\) with \(a_n\to x_0\) then \(f(a_n)+g(a_n)\to f(x_0)+g(x_0)\) as \(n\to\infty\text{.}\) Since both \(f\) and \(g\) are continuous at \(x_0\text{,}\) we have \(f(a_n)\to f(x_0)\) and \(g(a_n)\to g(x_0)\) as \(n\to\infty\text{.}\) Thus by the addition rule for sequential limits (Proposition 2.8) we have
\begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to \infty} \brac{f(a_n)+g(a_n)} = \lim_{n\to \infty} f(a_n)+\lim_{n\to \infty} g(a_n) = f(x_0) + g(x_0)\text{,} \end{equation*}
as required.

(b)

Define what it means to be a polynomial function, and prove that they are continuous.
Solution.
Definition 6.3. Polynomial.
Let \(D \subset \R\text{.}\) We say that a function \(f : D \to \R\) is a polynomial with real coefficients if there exist \(c_0, c_1, \dots, c_n \in \R\) such that for all \(x \in D\) we have
\begin{equation*} f(x) = c_0 + c_1 x + \dots + c_n x^n. \end{equation*}
We call \(n\) the degree of the polynomial.
Proposition 6.4.
Polynomials are continuous functions.
Proof.
The identity function \(x \mapsto x\) is continuous, since denoting this function by \(f\text{,}\) we have \(a_n \to x_0\) implies \(f(a_n) \to f(x_0)\text{.}\) By the multiplication rule (Proposition 2.10) it follows that \(x^2\) is continuous. Iterating this argument, we see that \(x^3 = x\cdot x^2\) is continuous. It follows from induction that \(x^n\) is continuous for any \(n \in \N\text{.}\) Since constant functions are continuous (Example 5.14), it again follows from the multiplication rule that \(c_n x^n\) is continuous for any \(c_n \in \R\text{.}\) By the addition rule (Proposition 2.8), the sum
\begin{equation*} c_0 + c_1x+ \dots + c_n x^n \end{equation*}
is continuous. Hence polynomials are continuous.

(c)

Define what it means to be a rational function, and prove that they are continuous.
Solution.
Definition 6.5. Rational function.
Let \(D \subset \R\text{.}\) We say that \(f : D \to \R\) is a rational function if there exist polynomials \(P, Q : D\to \R\) such that: \((\forall x \in D)[Q(x) \neq 0]\text{;}\) \((\forall x \in D)\sqbrac{f(x) = \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}}\text{.}\)
Proposition 6.6.
Rational functions are continuous.
Proof.
This follows from the fact that polynomials are continuous and the fact that continuity is preserved on taking quotients, provided that the denominator is non-zero (Proposition 6.2). The fact that the denominator is not zero at any point in the domain of a rational function follows from Definition 6.5 (the rational function would not be defined at a point at which the denominator is zero).

(d)

State and prove how continuity is preserved under composition of functions.
Solution.
Proposition 6.7. Composition preserves continuity.
Let \(f_1 : D_1 \to D_2\) with \(D_1, D_2 \subset \R\text{.}\) Let \(f_2 : D_2 \to \R\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f_1\) is continuous at \(x_0 \in D_1\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f_2\) is continuous at \(f_1(x_0)\text{.}\) Then the composition 1 \(f_2 \circ f_1\) is continuous at \(x_0\text{.}\)
Proof.
Let \((a_n)_n\) be a sequence in \(D_1\) which tends to \(x_0\text{.}\) By definition of the function limit (Definition 5.11), we are required to prove that \(f_2 \circ f_1(a_n)\to f_2 \circ f_1(x_0)\) as \(n\to\infty\text{.}\) Since \(f_1\) is continuous at \(x_0\) we have \(f_1(a_n)\to f_1(x_0)\) as \(n\to\infty\text{.}\) The continuity of \(f_2\) at \(f_1(x_0)\) then gives that \(f_2 \brac{f_1(a_n)}\to f_2 \brac{ f_1(x_0)}\) as \(n\to\infty\text{,}\) as required.

(e)

State and prove how continuity is preserved under taking absolute values and min/max.
Solution.
Proposition 6.8.
Let \(f : D \to \R\) with \(D \subset \R\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f\) is continuous at \(x_0 \in D\text{.}\) Then \(|f|\) is continuous at \(x_0 \in D\text{.}\)
Proof.
This follows from the fact that continuity is preserved under composition of functions (Proposition 6.7), and the fact that the absolute value function \(x\mapsto |x|\) is continuous on \(\R\) (Example 5.16).
Proposition 6.9. Min/max of two continuous functions is continuous.
Let \(f, g: D \to \R\) be real valued functions on a real domain \(D \subset \R\text{.}\) Suppose that both \(f\) and \(g\) are continuous at \(x_0 \in D\text{.}\) Then both \(\min\set{f,g}\) and \(\max\set{f,g}\) are continuous at \(x_0 \in D\text{.}\)
Proof.
We first observe that
\begin{equation*} \max\set{f, 0} = \frac{ f+ |f|}{2}. \end{equation*}
The latter function is continuous at \(x_0\) on combining Proposition 6.2 and Proposition 6.8. Next observe that
\begin{equation*} \max\set{f,g}= \max\set{f-g,0} + g. \end{equation*}
The latter function is continuous at \(x_0\) on combining our previous observation with the fact that the sum of two continuous functions is continuous (Proposition 6.2). The argument for \(\min\) is similar.

Lecture 6.10. Uniform continuity.

(a)

Define what it means for a real-valued function on a real domain to be uniformly continuous.
Solution.
Definition 6.11. Uniformly continuous.
Let \(f : D \to \R\) be a real valued function on a real domain \(D \subset \R\text{.}\) We say that \(f\) is uniformly continuous on \(D\) if
\begin{align} (\forall \eps \gt 0)(\exists \delta = \delta(\eps) \gt 0)\amp (\forall x_1,x_2 \in D)\notag\\ \amp\bigsqbrac{\ |x_1-x_2| \leq \delta \implies |f(x_1)-f(x_2)| \leq \eps\ }.\tag{6.1} \end{align}

(b)

Justify why uniform continuity implies continuity, but not vice versa.
Solution.
Remark 6.12. Uniform continuity implies continuity.
It is important to compare the definition of uniform continuity (6.1) with that of continuity (5.5), the latter being:
\begin{align} (\forall x_0 \in D)(\forall \eps \gt 0)(\exists \delta = \delta(x_0,\eps) \gt 0)\amp (\forall x \in D)\notag\\ \bigsqbrac{\ |x-x_0| \leq \delta \implies |f(x)-f(x_0)| \leq \eps\ }.\tag{6.2} \end{align}
The only difference is the order of the quantifiers: for continuity (6.2) the parameter \(\delta= \delta(x_0, \eps)\) is allowed to depend on the point of continuity \(x_0\text{,}\) however for uniformity continuity (6.1) the same \(\delta = \delta(\eps)\) has to work for all points \(x_0\) in the domain \(D\text{.}\) If \(f\) is uniformly continuous then it is therefore continuous, on taking \(\delta(x_0, \eps) := \delta(\eps)\) for any \(x_0 \in D\text{.}\) However, the converse 2  is not always true, as the next example illustrates.
Example 6.13. Continuity does not imply uniform continuity.
Define \(f : \R \to \R\) at each \(x \in \R\) by \(f(x) := x^2\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is a polynomial, so is continuous on \(\R\) by Proposition 6.4. By way of contradiction, suppose that \(f\) were uniformly continuous on \(\R\text{.}\) Taking \(\eps := 1\) in the definition of uniform continuity (Definition 6.11), there exists \(\delta \gt 0\) such that if \(|x_1 - x_2| \leq \delta\) then \(|x_1^2 - x_2^2| \leq 1\text{.}\) Setting \(x_2 := x_1 + \delta\text{,}\) we deduce that for any \(x_1 \gt 0\) we have
\begin{equation*} 1 \geq |x_1^2 - x_2^2| = |x_1-x_2||x_1+x_2| = \delta (2x_1+\delta) \geq \delta x_1. \end{equation*}
Taking, say, \(x_1 = 2/\delta\) then yields a contradiction. The domain of a function is important for uniform continuity: the function \(f(x) = x^2\) is not uniformly continuous on \(\R\text{,}\) yet one can show that it is uniformly continuous on any closed interval \([a,b]\) (see Theorem 6.14).

(c)

State and prove how a continuous function on a closed interval is uniformly continuous.
Solution.
Theorem 6.14. Continuous on closed interval implies uniformly continuous.
Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be a real valued function on a closed interval\([a,b] \subset \R\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is uniformly continuous on \([a,b]\text{.}\)
Proof.
By way of contradiction, suppose that \(f\) is not uniformly continuous on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Negating the definition of uniform continuity (6.1), we deduce that
\begin{equation*} (\exists \eps \gt 0)(\forall \delta \gt 0)(\exists x,y \in [a,b]) \bigsqbrac{\ |x-y| \leq \delta \quad \text{and} \quad |f(x)-f(y)| \gt \eps\ }. \end{equation*}
Taking \(\delta := 1/n\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} (\forall n \in \N)(\exists x_n,y_n \in [a,b]) \bigsqbrac{\ |x_n-y_n| \leq \trecip{n} \quad \text{and} \quad |f(x_n)-f(y_n)| \gt \eps\ }. \end{equation*}
By the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem (Corollary 3.14) and the inequality rule for limits (Proposition 2.2) there exists \(x_0\in [a,b]\) and \(k(1) \lt k(2) \lt \dots\) such that \(x_{k(n)} \to x_0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\) Applying the same argument to the sequence \((y_{k(n)})_n\text{,}\) there exists \(y_0\in [a,b]\) and \(\ell_1 \lt \ell_2 \lt \dots\) such that \(y_{k(\ell_n)} \to y_0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\) Since subsequences of convergent sequence tend to the same limit (Proposition 3.11), we also have \(x_{k(\ell_n)} \to x_0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\) Notice that
\begin{align*} |x_0 - y_0| \amp \leq |x_0-x_{k(\ell_n)}| + |x_{k(\ell_n)}- y_{k(\ell_n)}| + |y_{k(\ell_n)} -y_0|\\ \amp \leq |x_0-x_{k(\ell_n)}| + \recip{k(\ell_n)} + |y_{k(\ell_n)} -y_0|\\ \amp \to 0 \quad \text{as}\quad n \to \infty. \end{align*}
Therefore \(x_0 = y_0\text{.}\) Yet continuity of \(f\text{,}\) continuity of the absolute value and the addition rule (Proposition 2.8) together give that
\begin{equation*} \eps \lt |f(x_{k(\ell_n)}) - f(y_{k(\ell_n)})| \to |f(x_0)-f(y_0)| = 0. \end{equation*}
Since this is a contradiction, it follows that \(f\) must indeed be uniformly continuous.

Lecture 6.15. The intermediate value theorem.

(a)

State and prove the intermediate value theorem.
Solution.
Theorem 6.16. Intermediate value theorem.
Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be a continuous function on a closed interval \([a,b] \subset \R\text{.}\) For any real number \(y\) between 3 \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\text{,}\) there exists \(x \in [a,b]\) such that \(f(x) = y\text{.}\)
Proof.

We prove the result assuming that \(f(a) \leq y \leq f(b)\text{;}\) if \(f(a) \geq y \geq f(b)\) then we reduce to the former case on considering \(-f\text{.}\) Set

\begin{equation*} x := \sup\set{t \in [a,b] : f(t) \leq y}. \end{equation*}

The above supremum exists because the set is non-empty (it contains \(a\)) and is bounded above by \(b\text{.}\) In particular, \(a \leq x \leq b\text{.}\) We claim that \(f(x) = y\text{.}\)

We first observe that for any \(n\in \N\) the number \(x-1/n\) is smaller than the supremum, so cannot be an upper bound. Hence there exists \(t_n \in [a,b]\) with \(f(t_n) \leq y\) and \(x-1/n \lt t_n \leq x\text{.}\) By the sandwich rule we have \(t_n \to x\) as \(n\to\infty\text{,}\) so by continuity and the inequality rule, we get that \(f(x)= \lim_{n\to \infty} f(t_n) \leq y\text{.}\)

It remains to show that \(f(x_0) \geq y\text{.}\) By way of contradiction, suppose that \(f(x_0) \lt y\text{.}\) Then \(x_0 \lt b\) (since \(f(b) \geq y \)). Continuous functions which are positive at a point are positive on a neighbourhood of that point (see Proposition 5.20). Hence there exists \(\delta \gt 0\) such that for all \(x\in [a,b]\cap(x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)\) we have \(y-f(x) \gt 0\text{.}\) On setting

\begin{equation*} \eps := \min\set{b-x_0, \delta/2} \gt 0, \end{equation*}

we obtain \(x+\eps \in [a,b]\) with \(f(x+\eps) \leq y\text{.}\) Yet, since \(x+\eps\) exceeds \(x\text{,}\) this contradicts the definition of \(x\) as the least upper bound of the set of \(t \in [a,b]\) with \(f(t) \leq y\text{.}\)

(b)

Use the intermediate value theorem to state and prove the existence of positive \(n\)th roots.
Solution.
Corollary 6.17. Existence of roots.
Let \(n \in \N\text{.}\) Let \(a \in [0, \infty)\text{.}\) Then there exists a unique \(x \in [0, \infty)\) such that
\begin{equation*} x^n = a. \end{equation*}
Proof.
The function \(x \mapsto x^n\) is continuous on the closed interval \([0, 1+a]\text{,}\) by Proposition 6.4. One can check that
\begin{equation*} 0^n \leq a \leq (1+a)^n. \end{equation*}
Hence by the intermediate value theorem (Theorem 6.16), there exists \(x \in [0, 1+a]\) such that \(x^n = a\text{.}\) For uniqueness, suppose that \(\tilde x \in [0,\infty)\) also satisfies \(\tilde x^n = a\text{.}\) Factorising, we have
\begin{equation*} (x-\tilde x)(x^{n-1} + x^{n-2}\tilde x + \dots + x \tilde x^{n-2} + \tilde x^{n-1}) = x^n - \tilde x^n = 0. \end{equation*}
If \(x \neq \tilde x\) then the factor \(x^{n-1} + \dots + \tilde x^{n-1}\) must be zero. Since \(x, \tilde x \geq 0\text{,}\) the only way this can happen is if \(x = \tilde x\text{.}\)
Definition 6.18. \(n\)th root function.
Let \(n \in \N\text{.}\) We define the function \(\sqrt[n]{} : [0, \infty) \to [0, \infty)\) by setting \(\sqrt[n]{a}\) to be the unique non-negative real number \(x \in [0, \infty)\) such that \(x^n= a\text{.}\) The existence and uniqueness of \(x\) are guaranteed by Corollary 6.17.

Lecture 6.19. The extreme value theorem.

(a)

State and prove the extreme value theorem.
Solution.
Theorem 6.20. Extreme value theorem.
Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be a continuous function. Then there exist \(x_1,x_2 \in [a,b]\) such that
\begin{equation*} f(x_1) = \inf_{[a,b]} f \quad \text{and} \quad f(x_2) = \sup_{[a,b]} f. \end{equation*}
In particular, a continuous function on a closed interval is bounded.
Proof.
We prove the result for the supremum, the argument for the infimum being similar. Notice that the supremum \(\sup_{[a,b]} f\) is either real or equal to \(\infty\text{.}\) We first discount the latter situation. If \(\sup_{[a,b]} f = \infty\) then for any \(n \in \N\) there exist \(x_n \in [a,b]\) with \(f(x_n) \gt n\) (otherwise \(n\) is a finite upper bound for \(f\)). By the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem, there exists \(k_1 \lt k_2 \lt \dots\) such that the subsequence \(x_{k_n}\) tends to a real limit \(x_0\) for some \(x_0 \in \R\text{.}\) By the inequality rule (Proposition 2.2) we have \(x_0 \in [a,b]\text{.}\) By continuity (Definition 5.13) and the inequality rule (Proposition 2.2) we have
\begin{equation*} f(x_0) = \lim_{n \to \infty} f(x_{k_n}) \geq \lim_{n \to \infty} k_n = \infty. \end{equation*}
This is a contradiction, since \(f(x_0)\) is a real number. It follows that the supremum is finite. Since decreasing a supremum gives a non upper bound (Proposition 1.7), for each \(n \in \N\) there exists \(x_n \in [a,b]\) such that
\begin{equation*} \brac{\sup_{[a,b]} f}-\recip n \lt f(x_n) \leq \sup_{[a,b]} f. \end{equation*}
Since the set \([a,b]\) is bounded, the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem (Corollary 3.14) gives the existence of \(k_1 \lt k_2 \lt k_3 \lt \dots\) such that the subsequence \(x_{k_n}\) converges to some \(x_0 \in [a,b]\text{.}\) Since \(f\) is continuous at \(x_0\text{,}\) the sequential definition of continuity (Definition 5.13) gives that
\begin{equation*} f(x_0) = \lim_{n\to \infty} f(x_{k_n}). \end{equation*}
By the sandwich rule (Proposition 2.6), we deduce that
\begin{equation*} f(x_0) = \sup_{[a,b]} f. \end{equation*}

Lecture 6.21. Differentiability.

(a)

Define what it means for a real-valued function on an open interval to be differentiable at a point and define the derivative at that point. State what it means for such a function to be differentiable.
Solution.
Definition 6.22. Differentiable.
Let \(f : (a,b)\to \R\) and \(x_0 \in (a,b)\text{.}\) We say that \(f\) is differentiable at \(x_0\) if
\begin{align} (\exists y \in \R)(\forall \eps \gt 0)\amp(\exists \delta\gt 0)(\forall x \in (a,b))\notag\\ \amp\sqbrac{0\lt |x-x_0| \leq \delta \implies \abs{\frac{f(x)-f(x_0)}{x-x_0}-y} \leq \eps}.\tag{6.3} \end{align}
Should such a value of \(y\) exist, one can check that it is unique, and we call it the derivative of \(f\) at \(x_0\text{,}\) denoted \(f'(x_0)\text{.}\)

(b)

State and prove the "sequential characterisation" of differentiability.
Solution.
Proposition 6.23. Sequential characterisation of differentiability.
Let \(f : (a,b) \to \R\) and \(x_0 \in (a,b)\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is differentiable at \(x_0\) with derivative \(y\) if and only if for any sequence \((a_n)_n\) in \((a,b)\setminus\set{x_0}\) with \(a_n\to x_0\) we have
\begin{equation} \frac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}{a_n-x_0} \to y \text{ as } n \to \infty\text{.}\tag{6.4} \end{equation}
Proof.

This is essentially the same argument as that given in the proof of Proposition 5.19.

We first prove the "\(\implies\)" direction. Let \((a_n)_n\) be a sequence in \((a,b)\setminus\set{x_0}\) such that \(a_n \to x_0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\) We are required to prove (6.4), assuming (6.3). Fix \(\eps \gt0\text{.}\) Then by (6.3) there exists \(\delta = \delta(\eps) \gt 0\) such that if \(x \in (a,b)\) and \(0\lt |x-x_0| \leq \delta\) then

\begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{f(x)-f(x_0)}{x-x_0} - y}\leq \eps\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(a_n \to x_0\) there exists \(N = N(\delta)\) such that for all \(n\geq N\) we have \(0\lt |a_n - x_0| \leq \delta\text{.}\) Hence if \(n \geq N(\delta)\) then

\begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}{a_n-x_0} - y}\leq \eps\text{.} \end{equation*}

This establishes (6.4).

Next we prove the "\(\impliedby\)" direction. To do so we prove the contrapositive. Hence, suppose that the negation of (6.3) holds:

\begin{align*} (\exists \eps \gt 0)(\forall \delta \gt 0)\amp(\exists x= x(\delta) \in (a,b))\\ \bigsqbrac{\ 0\lt |x-x_0| \leq \delta\quad \text{and}\quad \abs{\frac{f(x)-f(x_0)}{x-x_0} - y} \gt \eps\ }\text{.} \end{align*}

Taking \(\delta = 1/n\) in the above negation, set \(a_1:= x(1)\text{,}\) \(a_2:=x(1/2)\text{,}\) \(a_3 := x(1/3)\text{,}\) etc. Then \(a_n \in (a,b)\setminus\set{x_0}\text{,}\) \(|a_n - x_0| \leq 1/n\) and

\begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}{a_n-x_0} - y} \geq \eps\text{.} \end{equation*}

Hence \((a_n)_n\) is a sequence in \((a,b)\setminus\set{x_0}\) with \(a_n \to x_0\) and

\begin{equation*} \frac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}{a_n-x_0} \not\to y\text{,} \end{equation*}

(as required).

(c)

State and prove how a function which is differentiable at a point is continuous at that point.
Solution.
Proposition 6.24. Differentiable implies continuous.
Let \(f : (a,b) \to \R\) be differentiable at \(x_0 \in (a,b)\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is continuous at \(x_0\text{.}\)
Proof.
By the epsilon-delta definition of continuity (see Proposition 5.19) we are required to verify that
\begin{align} (\forall \eps \gt 0)(\exists \delta_1 = \delta_1(x_0,\eps) \gt 0)\amp(\forall x \in (a,b))\notag\\ \amp \bigsqbrac{\ |x-x_0| \leq \delta_1 \implies |f(x)-f(x_0)| \leq \eps\ }.\tag{6.5} \end{align}
By the definition of differentiability (see Definition 6.22) we have that
\begin{align*} (\forall \eps \gt 0)(\exists \delta_2 = \delta_2(x_0,\eps) \gt 0)\amp (\forall x \in (a,b))\\ \sqbrac{\ 0\lt |x-x_0| \leq \delta_2 \implies \abs{\frac{f(x)-f(x_0)}{x-x_0}-f'(x_0)} \leq \eps\ }. \end{align*}
In particular, if \(x \in (a,b) \) and \(0\lt|x-x_0| \leq \delta_2\) then
\begin{equation*} \abs{f(x)-f(x_0)} \leq \eps \abs{x-x_0} + \abs{f'(x_0)}|x-x_0|. \end{equation*}
Let us assume that \(f'(x_0) \neq 0\text{,}\) the remaining case being simpler. We therefore obtain (6.5) on taking
\begin{equation*} \delta_1(\eps) := \min\set{\delta_2(\eps), \recip{2}, \frac{\eps}{2\abs{f'(x_0)}}}. \end{equation*}

(d)

Give examples (with proof) of differentiable and non-differentiable functions.
Solution.
Example 6.25. Constant functions are differentiable.
Let \(f : (a,b) \to \R\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f\) is constant. In other words, there exists \(c \in \R\) such that for all \(x \in (a,b)\) we have \(f(x) = c\text{.}\) Then for \(h\neq 0\) and \(x \in (a,b)\) we have
\begin{equation*} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} = \frac{c-c}{h} = 0 \to 0 \quad \text{as} \quad h \to 0. \end{equation*}
Hence \(f\) is differentiable on \((a,b)\text{.}\) Moreover \(f' = 0\) on \((a,b)\text{.}\)
Example 6.26. The identity function is differentiable.
Let \(f : (a,b) \to \R\) be given by \(f(x):=x\) for all \(x \in (a,b)\text{.}\) Then for \(h\neq 0\) and \(x \in (a,b)\) we have
\begin{equation*} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} = \frac{(x+h)-x}{h} = 1 \to 1 \quad \text{as} \quad h \to 0. \end{equation*}
Hence \(f\) is differentiable on \((a,b)\text{.}\) Moreover \(f' =1\) on \((a,b)\text{.}\)
Example 6.27. The reciprocal function is differentiable.
Let \(f : \R\setminus\set{0} \to \R\) be given by \(f(x):=1/x\) for all \(x \in \R\setminus\set{0}\text{.}\) Then for \(h\neq 0\) and \(x \in \R\setminus\set{0}\) we have
\begin{equation*} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} = \frac{\recip{x+h}-\recip{x}}{h} = -\recip{(x+h)x} \to -\recip{x^2} \quad \text{as} \quad h \to 0. \end{equation*}
Hence \(f\) is differentiable on \(\R\setminus\set{0}\text{.}\) Moreover \(f'(x) =-1/x^2\) on \(\R\setminus\set{0}\text{.}\)
Since differentiable functions are continuous, any example of a non-continuous function is also an example of a non-differentiable function. For instance, the function Example 5.17 is discontinuous at \(x=0\text{,}\) so is not differentiable at \(x=0\text{.}\) Similarly, Example 5.18 is discontinuous everywhere, so is also nowhere differentiable.

(e)

Give an example (with proof) of a function which is continuous at a point, but not differentiable there.
Solution.
Example 6.28. A continuous function which is not differentiable.
The function \(x \mapsto |x|\) is an example of a function which is continuous at a point, but not differentiable there (namely at \(x_0 = 0\)). Hence continuity does not imply differentiability. To see that this function is not differentiable at \(x_0=0\text{,}\) we must prove the negation of Definition 6.22, namely
\begin{align} (\forall y \in \R)(\exists \eps \gt 0)(\forall \delta\gt 0)\amp(\exists x \in \R)\notag\\ \amp\sqbrac{0\lt |x-0| \leq \delta \text{ and } \abs{\frac{|x|-|0|}{x-0}-y} \gt \eps}\text{.}\tag{6.6} \end{align}
Simplifying, we have
\begin{equation*} \frac{|x|-|0|}{x-0} = \begin{cases} 1 \amp \text{ if } x \gt 0,\\ -1\amp \text{ if } x \lt 0.\end{cases} \end{equation*}
Hence, for fixed \(y\in \R\) we let \(\eps := 1/2\) and for any \(\delta > 0\) we let
\begin{equation*} x = x(y,\delta) = \begin{cases} -\delta \amp \text{ if } y \geq 0,\\ \delta \amp \text{ if } y \lt 0.\end{cases} \end{equation*}
Then \(|x-0| = \delta\) and
\begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{|x|-|0|}{x-0}-y} = 1+|y| \geq 1\gt \eps\text{.} \end{equation*}
This yields (6.6)
