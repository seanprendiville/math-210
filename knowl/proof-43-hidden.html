We prove the result for the supremum, the argument for the infimum being similar. Notice that the supremum \(\sup_{[a,b]} f\) is either real or equal to \(\infty\text{.}\) We first discount the latter situation. If \(\sup_{[a,b]} f = \infty\) then for any \(n \in \N\) there exist \(x_n \in [a,b]\) with \(f(x_n) \gt n\) (otherwise \(n\) is a finite upper bound for \(f\)). By the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem, there exists \(k_1 \lt k_2 \lt \dots\) such that the subsequence \(x_{k_n}\) tends to a real limit \(x_0\) for some \(x_0 \in \R\text{.}\) By the inequality rule (Proposition 2.2) we have \(x_0 \in [a,b]\text{.}\) By continuity (Definition 5.13) and the inequality rule (Proposition 2.2) we have
\begin{equation*} f(x_0) = \lim_{n \to \infty} f(x_{k_n}) \geq \lim_{n \to \infty} k_n = \infty. \end{equation*}
This is a contradiction, since \(f(x_0)\) is a real number. It follows that the supremum is finite. Since decreasing a supremum gives a non upper bound (Proposition 1.7), for each \(n \in \N\) there exists \(x_n \in [a,b]\) such that
\begin{equation*} \brac{\sup_{[a,b]} f}-\recip n \lt f(x_n) \leq \sup_{[a,b]} f. \end{equation*}
Since the set \([a,b]\) is bounded, the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem (Corollary 3.14) gives the existence of \(k_1 \lt k_2 \lt k_3 \lt \dots\) such that the subsequence \(x_{k_n}\) converges to some \(x_0 \in [a,b]\text{.}\) Since \(f\) is continuous at \(x_0\text{,}\) the sequential definition of continuity (Definition 5.13) gives that
\begin{equation*} f(x_0) = \lim_{n\to \infty} f(x_{k_n}). \end{equation*}
By the sandwich rule (Proposition 2.6), we deduce that
\begin{equation*} f(x_0) = \sup_{[a,b]} f. \end{equation*}
