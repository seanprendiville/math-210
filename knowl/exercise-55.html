- 7.9. Rules for differentiation.

(a)

State and prove both the addition and product rules for differentiation.
Solution.
Proposition 7.10. Algebra of differentiation.
Let \(f,g: (a,b)\to \R\) be differentiable at \(x_0 \in (a,b)\text{.}\) Then the sum \(f+g\) and product \(f\cdot g\) are differentiable at \(x_0\text{.}\) Moreover, their derivatives satisfy the identities
\begin{align*} (f+g)'(x_0) \amp = f'(x_0) + g'(x_0), \amp \text{(addition rule)}\\ (f\cdot g)'(x_0) \amp = f'(x_0)g(x_0) + f(x_0)g'(x_0).\amp \text{(product rule)} \end{align*}
Proof.
We verify both identities using the sequential characterisation of differentiability ([cross-reference to target(s) "prop-seq-diff" missing or not unique]) together with the addition and product rules for sequence limits (Proposition 2.14 and Proposition 2.17). To this end, let \((a_n)_n\) be a sequence in \((a,b)\setminus\set{x_0}\) with \(a_n \to x_0\text{.}\) Then the addition rule follows from the addition rule for sequence limits, since
\begin{align*} \lim_{n \to \infty} \amp \frac{(f+g)(a_n)-(f+g)(x_0)}{a_n-x_0}\\ \amp = \lim_{n\to \infty} \frac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}{a_n-x_0}+ \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)}{x-x_0}. \end{align*}
The product rule uses the identity
\begin{align*} \amp f(a_n)g(a_n) - f(x_0)g(x_0) = \sqbrac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}g(a_n) + f(x_0)\sqbrac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)}\\ \amp =\sqbrac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}\sqbrac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)} + \sqbrac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}g(x_0)+ f(x_0)\sqbrac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)}. \end{align*}
By the multiplication rule for sequence limits (Proposition 2.17), we have
\begin{gather*} \lim_{n \to \infty}\frac{\sqbrac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}g(x_0)}{a_n-x_0} = f'(x_0)g(x_0),\\ \lim_{n \to \infty}\frac{f(x_0)\sqbrac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)}}{a_n-x_0} = f(x_0)g'(x_0). \end{gather*}
Similarly
\begin{align*} \amp \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\sqbrac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}\sqbrac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)}}{a_n-x_0}\\ \amp = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\sqbrac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}}{a_n-x_0}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\sqbrac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)}}{a_n-x_0}\lim_{n\to\infty}(a_n-x_0)\\ \amp = f'(x_0)\cdot g'(x_0)\cdot 0 = 0. \end{align*}

(b)

State and prove the chain rule for differentiation.
Solution.
Theorem 7.11. Chain rule.
Let \(f : (a,b) \to (c,d)\) and \(g: (c,d) \to \R\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f\) is differentiable at \(x_0\) and that \(g\) is differentiable at \(f(x_0)\text{.}\) Then the composition \(g\circ f\) is differentiable at \(x_0\text{.}\) Moreover the derivative satisfies the identity
\begin{equation*} (g\circ f)'(x_0) = g'\bigbrac{f(x_0)} \cdot f'(x_0). \end{equation*}
Proof.
Let \((x_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be a sequence in \((a,b) \setminus\set{x_0}\) with \(x_n \to x_0\text{.}\) By [cross-reference to target(s) "prop-seq-diff" missing or not unique], we wish to prove that
\begin{equation} \lim_{n\to \infty} \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{x_n-x_0} = g'\bigbrac{f(x_0)}f'(x_0).\tag{7.2} \end{equation}
We have
\begin{equation} \frac{g\bigbrac{f( x_n)} - g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{ x_n-x_0} = \begin{cases} 0, \amp \text{if } f( x_n) = f(x_0);\\ \frac{g\brac{f( x_n)}-g\brac{f(x_0)}}{f( x_n)-f(x_0)}\cdot \frac{f( x_n)-f(x_0)}{ x_n-x_0}, \amp \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}\tag{7.3} \end{equation}
First, let us suppose that \(f(x_n) = f(x_0)\) for only finitely many values of \(n\text{.}\) Then there exists \(N_0\in \N\) such that for all \(n \geq N_0\) we have \(f(x_n)\neq f(x_0)\text{.}\) Hence for all \(n \geq N_0\) we have
\begin{equation*} \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{x_n-x_0} = \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}\cdot \frac{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}{x_n-x_0} . \end{equation*}
Since differentiability implies continuity (Proposition 7.7), we have
\begin{equation*} \lim_{n \to \infty} f(x_n) = f(x_0). \end{equation*}
Thus by the sequential characterisation of differentiability ([cross-reference to target(s) "prop-seq-diff" missing or not unique]) we have
\begin{equation} \lim_{\substack{n\to \infty\\ n \geq N_0}} \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{f(x_n)-f(x_0)} = g'\bigbrac{f(x_0)}.\tag{7.4} \end{equation}
We may therefore use the multiplication rule for sequential limits (Proposition 2.17) to deduce that
\begin{align*} \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{x_n-x_0} \amp = \lim_{\substack{n\to \infty\\ n \geq N_0}}\brac{ \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}\cdot \frac{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}{x_n-x_0}}\\ \amp = \lim_{\substack{n\to \infty\\ n \geq N_0}}\frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}\lim_{n\to \infty} \frac{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}{x_n-x_0}\\ \amp = g'\bigbrac{f(x_0)}f'(x_0). \end{align*}
Next, let us suppose that \(f(x_n) = f(x_0)\) for infinitely many values of \(n\text{.}\) Write \(k_1 \lt k_2 \lt \dots\) for those indices for which \(f(x_{k_n}) = f(x_0)\text{.}\) Write \(\ell_1 \lt \ell_2 \lt \dots\) for the (possibly finite) remaining indices with \(f(x_{l_n}) \neq f(x_0)\text{.}\) The sequence
\begin{equation} n\mapsto \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_{\ell_n})}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{f(x_{\ell_n})-f(x_0)}\tag{7.5} \end{equation}
is either a finite sequence, or converges as in the proof of (7.4). In either case, the sequence is bounded, so there exists \(M \in \R\) such that for all \(\ell_n\) we have
\begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_{\ell_n})}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{f(x_{\ell_n})-f(x_0)} } \leq M. \end{equation*}
Combining this with (7.3), for any \(n \in \N\) we have
\begin{equation} \abs{\frac{g\bigbrac{f( x_n)} - g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{ x_n-x_0}} \leq M\abs{\frac{f(x_{n}) - f(x_0)}{x_{n} - x_0}}.\tag{7.6} \end{equation}
Since \(x_{k_n} \to x_0\) we have
\begin{equation*} f'(x_0) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{f(x_{k_n}) - f(x_0)}{x_{k_n} - x_0} = 0. \end{equation*}
So it follows that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{f(x_{n}) - f(x_0)}{x_{n} - x_0} = f'(x_0) = 0. \end{equation*}
Combining this with (7.6) gives
\begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to \infty}\abs{\frac{g\bigbrac{f( x_n)} - g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{ x_n-x_0}} = 0. \end{equation*}
Hence (7.2) also follows in this case.

(c)

State and prove the quotient rule for differentiation.
Solution.
Corollary 7.12. Quotient rule.
Let \(f,g : (a,b) \to \R\) both be differentiable at \(x_0 \in D\text{.}\) Suppose that \(g(x_0) \neq 0\text{.}\) Then the quotient \(f/g\) is defined on \((x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)\) for some \(\delta \gt 0\) and is differentiable at \(x_0\text{.}\) Moreover the derivative satisfies the identity
\begin{equation} (f/g)'(x_0) = \frac{f'(x_0)g(x_0)-f(x_0)g'(x_0)}{g(x_0)^2}.\tag{7.7} \end{equation}
Proof.
Since differentiability implies continuity (Proposition 7.7), the function \(g\) is continuous at \(x_0\text{.}\) Since \(g(x_0) \neq 0\text{,}\) and non-zero points of continuity yield a non-zero neighbourhood (Proposition 5.19), there exists \(\delta \gt0\) such that \(g(x) \neq 0\) for all \(x \in (a,b)\cap (x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)\text{.}\) Decreasing \(\delta\) if necessary, we may assume that \((x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)\subset (a,b)\text{.}\) Hence the quotient \(x \mapsto f(x)/g(x)\) is defined on \((x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)\text{.}\) Composing the reciprocal function \(y \mapsto 1/y\) with \(g\text{,}\) the chain rule (Theorem 7.11) and Example 7.5 together give that the function
\begin{equation*} x\mapsto \recip{g(x)} \end{equation*}
is differentiable at \(x_0\text{.}\) Moreover, the derivative satisfies
\begin{equation*} \brac{\recip{g(x_0)}}' = -\frac{g'(x_0)}{g(x_0)^2}. \end{equation*}
Hence by the product rule (Proposition 7.10) the quotient \(f/g = f \cdot\brac{1/g}\) is differentiable at \(x_0\) with derivative
\begin{equation*} \frac{f'(x_0)}{g(x_0)} - \frac{f(x_0)g'(x_0)}{g(x_0)^2}. \end{equation*}
This completes the proof of the quotient rule.

(d)

Using the above, justify why rational functions are differentiable.
Solution.
Example 7.13. Polynomials and rational functions are differentiable.
Since the identity function \(x \mapsto x\) is differentiable on \(\R\text{,}\) the product rule gives that \(x \mapsto x^2\) is differentiable on \(\R\text{.}\) Using induction and the product rule, it follows that the for any \(n \in \N\) the function \(x \mapsto x^n = x\cdot x^{n-1}\) is differentiable on \(\R\) with derivative
\begin{equation*} (x^n)' = nx^{n-1}. \end{equation*}
Since constant functions \(x \mapsto c\) are differentiable on \(\R\text{,}\) with zero derivative, it follows from the product rule that the function \(x \mapsto c x^n\) is differentiable on \(\R\) with derivative
\begin{equation*} (cx^n)' = nc x^{n-1}. \end{equation*}
By the addition rule for differentiation (Proposition 7.10) it follows that any polynomial
\begin{equation*} x \mapsto c_0 + c_1 x + \dots + c_n x^n \end{equation*}
is differentiable on \(\R\text{,}\) with derivative
\begin{equation*} c_1 + 2c_2x + \dots + n c_n x^{n-1}. \end{equation*}
By the quotient rule for differentiation (Corollary 7.12) we deduce that any rational function (see [cross-reference to target(s) "def-rational" missing or not unique]) is differentiable on its domain of definition.
