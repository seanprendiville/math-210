By way of contradiction, suppose that \(f\) is not uniformly continuous on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Negating the definition of uniform continuity (6.1), we deduce that
\begin{equation*} (\exists \eps \gt 0)(\forall \delta \gt 0)(\exists x,y \in [a,b]) \bigsqbrac{\ |x-y| \leq \delta \quad \text{and} \quad |f(x)-f(y)| \gt \eps\ }. \end{equation*}
Taking \(\delta := 1/n\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} (\forall n \in \N)(\exists x_n,y_n \in [a,b]) \bigsqbrac{\ |x_n-y_n| \leq \trecip{n} \quad \text{and} \quad |f(x_n)-f(y_n)| \gt \eps\ }. \end{equation*}
By the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem (Corollary 3.14) and the inequality rule for limits (Proposition 2.2) there exists \(x_0\in [a,b]\) and \(k(1) \lt k(2) \lt \dots\) such that \(x_{k(n)} \to x_0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\) Applying the same argument to the sequence \((y_{k(n)})_n\text{,}\) there exists \(y_0\in [a,b]\) and \(\ell_1 \lt \ell_2 \lt \dots\) such that \(y_{k(\ell_n)} \to y_0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\) Since subsequences of convergent sequence tend to the same limit (Proposition 3.11), we also have \(x_{k(\ell_n)} \to x_0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\) Notice that
\begin{align*} |x_0 - y_0| \amp \leq |x_0-x_{k(\ell_n)}| + |x_{k(\ell_n)}- y_{k(\ell_n)}| + |y_{k(\ell_n)} -y_0|\\ \amp \leq |x_0-x_{k(\ell_n)}| + \recip{k(\ell_n)} + |y_{k(\ell_n)} -y_0|\\ \amp \to 0 \quad \text{as}\quad n \to \infty. \end{align*}
Therefore \(x_0 = y_0\text{.}\) Yet continuity of \(f\text{,}\) continuity of the absolute value and the addition rule (Proposition 2.8) together give that
\begin{equation*} \eps \lt |f(x_{k(\ell_n)}) - f(y_{k(\ell_n)})| \to |f(x_0)-f(y_0)| = 0. \end{equation*}
Since this is a contradiction, it follows that \(f\) must indeed be uniformly continuous.
