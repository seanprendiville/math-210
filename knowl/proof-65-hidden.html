First suppose that \(f\) is Riemann integrable. Since decreasing the supremum gives a non-upper bound (Proposition 1.7), there exists a subdivision \(a = y_0 \lt \dots \lt y_M = b\) such that

\begin{equation*} L(f;y_0, \dots, y_N) \geq \int_a^b f - \trecip{2}\eps. \end{equation*}

Since increasing the infimum gives a non lower bound (Proposition 1.7), there exists a subdivision \(a = z_0 \lt \dots \lt z_T = b\) such that

\begin{equation*} U(f;z_0, \dots, z_T) \leq \int_a^b f + \trecip{2}\eps. \end{equation*}

Let \(a= x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = b\) denote the common refinement of \((y_0, \dots, y_M)\) and \((z_0, \dots, z_T)\text{.}\) The required inequalities now follow from the monotonicty of Riemann sums with respect to refinements ( Proposition 8.21).

Next we prove the converse direction. Observe that for any subdivision \(a = x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N\) we have

\begin{equation*} U(f;[a,b]) \leq U(f;x_0, \dots, x_N) \quad \text{and} \quad L(f;[a,b]) \geq L(f;x_0, \dots, x_N) . \end{equation*}

Hence by our assumption, for any \(\eps \gt 0\) we have

\begin{equation*} U(f;[a,b]) -L(f;[a,b]) \leq \eps. \end{equation*}

Since this holds for all \(\eps \gt 0\) we deduce that

\begin{equation*} U(f;[a,b]) \leq L(f;[a,b]). \end{equation*}

The reverse inequality follows from (8.6). Hence \(U(f;[a,b]) = L(f;[a,b])\text{,}\) as required.
