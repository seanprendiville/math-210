Proposition 4.7. Series of non-negative terms always exist.
Let \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be a non-negative sequence, so that \(a_n \geq 0\) for all \(n\in \N\text{.}\) Then the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) exists, although it may equal \(\infty\text{.}\)
Proof.
Since \(a_n \geq 0\) for all \(n\in \N\text{,}\) the sequence of partial sums
\begin{equation*} a_1 \quad \leq\quad a_1 + a_2 \quad \leq\quad a_1 + a_2 + a_3 \quad \leq\quad \dots \end{equation*}
is an increasing sequence of real numbers. Hence by the monotone convergence principle (PropositionÂ 2.3), the limit
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n = \lim_{N\to \infty} \sum_{n=1}^Na_n \end{equation*}
always exists, but is possibly \(\infty\text{.}\)
