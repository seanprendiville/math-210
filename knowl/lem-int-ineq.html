Lemma 9.21. Integrability is preserved under taking positive parts.
Let \(a, b\in \R\) with \(a\lt b\text{.}\) Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be Riemann integrable. Then \(f^+\) is Riemann integrable on \([a,b]\text{:}\)
Proof.
Approximating the integral with a Riemann sum (see [cross-reference to target(s) "prop-eps-riemann" missing or not unique]), for any \(\eps \gt 0\) there exists a subdivision \(a = x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = b\) such that
\begin{equation*} U(f;x_0, \dots, x_N)-L(f;x_0, \dots, x_N) \leq \eps. \end{equation*}
We claim that
\begin{align} U(f^+;x_0, \dots, x_N)- \amp L(f^+;x_0, \dots, x_N)\notag\\ \amp \leq U(f;x_0, \dots, x_N)- L(f;x_0, \dots, x_N),\tag{9.3} \end{align}
from which it follows that \(f^+\) is integrable by the existence of arbitrarily close upper and lower Riemann sums ([cross-reference to target(s) "prop-eps-riemann" missing or not unique]). To establish our claim (9.3), we first observe that for \(x \geq 0\) and \(y \leq 0\) we have
\begin{equation} |x^+-y^+| = |x| = x \leq x+(-y) = |x-y|.\tag{9.4} \end{equation}
Checking the remaining cases, one may verify that \(|x^+-y^+| \leq |x-y|\) for any choice of \(x, y \in \R\text{.}\) Since decreasing a supremum gives a non upper bound (see LemmaÂ 1.9), for any \(\eps \gt 0\) there exist \(y \in [x_{n-1}, x_n]\) such that
\begin{equation*} \sup_{ [x_{n-1}, x_n]} f^+ - \eps \leq f^+(y) \leq \sup_{ [x_{n-1}, x_n]} f^+. \end{equation*}
Similarly there exists \(z \in [x_{n-1}, x_n]\) such that
\begin{equation*} \inf_{ [x_{n-1}, x_n]} f^+ \leq f^+(z) \leq \inf_{ [x_{n-1}, x_n]} f^+ + \eps. \end{equation*}
Hence by the triangle inequality and (9.4) we have
\begin{align*} \sup_{ [x_{n-1}, x_n]} f^+ - \inf_{ [x_{n-1}, x_n]} f^+ \amp \leq |f^+(y) - f^+(z)| +2\eps\\ \amp\leq |f(y)-f(z)| + 2\eps\\\\ \amp\leq \sup_{ [x_{n-1}, x_n]} f - \inf_{ [x_{n-1}, x_n]} f+2\eps. \end{align*}
Since this holds for arbitrary \(\eps \gt 0\text{,}\) we deduce (9.3).
