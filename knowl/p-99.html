Notice, however, that it is not the case that
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x} \to \infty \quad \text{as}\quad x\to 0 \quad \text{through}\quad \R\setminus\set{0}. \end{equation*}
This is because, for instance, the sequences \((1/n)_n\) and \((-1/n)_n\) are both contained in \(\R\setminus\set{0}\) and both tend to \(0\text{,}\) yet
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{1/n} \to +\infty \qquad \text{and}\qquad \frac{1}{-1/n} \to -\infty\text{.} \end{equation*}
