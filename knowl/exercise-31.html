- 4.1. Infinite series and convergence.

Given a sequence of real numbers, define the corresponding \(N\)th partial sum and infinite series. When do we say the infinite series exists? What is a term of an infinite series? What does it mean to say a series converges?
Solution.
Definition 4.2. Infinite series.
Given a sequence of real numbers \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) and \(N\in \N\text{,}\) the \(N\)th partial sum is given by
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^N a_n := a_1 + a_2 + \dots + a_N. \end{equation*}
If the sequence of partial sums tends to a limit as \(N \to \infty\text{,}\) then the corresponding infinite series exists and is equal to the limit
\begin{equation*} \lim_{N\to\infty} \sum_{n=1}^Na_n, \quad \text{written}\quad \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n. \end{equation*}
The terms of the infinite series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) are the summands \(a_n\text{.}\)
If the limit \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n=\lim_{N\to \infty} \sum_{n=1}^N a_n\) exists and equals a finite real number, then we say that the series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \) is convergent.
Example 4.3. Geometric series.
If \(|x|\lt 1\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n = \frac{1}{1-x}. \end{equation*}
Proof.
We have
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^N x^n = 1 + \brac{x\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x^n} = 1+ \brac{x\sum_{n=0}^N x^n}- x^{N+1} . \end{equation*}
Re-arranging (provided that \(x \neq 1\)) gives
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^N x^n = \frac{1-x^{N+1}}{1-x}. \end{equation*}
If \(|x| \lt 1\) then \(x^N \to 0\) as \(N\to \infty\) by Proposition 3.14 and Proposition 3.11. By the algebra of limits (Proposition 2.14, Proposition 2.17 and Proposition 2.21) we have
\begin{equation*} \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{n=0}^N x^n = \lim_{N \to \infty} \frac{1-x^{N+1}}{1-x} = \frac{1 - x\displaystyle\lim_{N\to \infty} x^N}{1-x} = \frac{1}{1-x}. \end{equation*}
Example 4.4. A series which does not converge but which exists.
Set \(a_n := 1/2\) for \(n=1, 2, \dots\text{.}\) Then the partial sums satisfy
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^N a_n = \frac{N}{2} \to \infty \quad \text{as } N \to \infty. \end{equation*}
Thus \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty 1/2\) exists, but is not convergent (it equals \(\infty\)).
Example 4.5. A series which does not exist.
Set \(a_n := (-1)^n\) for \(n=1, 2, \dots\text{.}\) Then the partial sums satisfy \(\sum_{n=1}^N a_n = -1\) if \(N\) is odd and \(\sum_{n=1}^N a_n = 0\) if \(N\) is even. It follows that the sequence of partial sums has a subsequence with limit \(-1\) and a subsequence with limit \(0\text{.}\) Therefore the sequence of partial sums cannot tend to a limit, since if a sequence has a limit all its subsequences tend to the same limit (Proposition 3.20). Thus \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^n\) does not exist.
