It remains to show that \(f(x) \leq y\text{.}\) By way of contradiction, suppose that \(f(x) \gt y\text{.}\) Since inequalities spread around a point of continuity (PropositionÂ 5.19), there exists \(\delta \gt 0\) such that for all \(t\in [a,b]\cap[x-\delta, x+\delta]\) we have \(f(x) \gt y\text{.}\) Notice that if \(a\geq x-\delta\text{,}\) then \(a \in [a,b]\cap[x-\delta, x+\delta]\) and hence \(f(a)\gt y\text{,}\) which is a contradiction. Thus \(a\lt x-\delta\text{.}\) In particular, \(x-\delta \in [a,b]\cap[x-\delta, x+\delta]\) and so \(f(x-\delta) \gt y\text{.}\) However the latter contradicts the fact that \(x\) is a lower bound for the set of \(t\in [a,b]\) satisfying \(f(t)\geq y\text{.}\) Thus \(f(x)\leq y\text{,}\) as required.
