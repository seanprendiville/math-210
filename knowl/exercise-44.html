- 5.25. True/false workshop quiz.

In your workshop, you will vote on whether you think each of the following statements is true (thumbs up) or false (thumbs down). For each question, your workshop tutor will ask one of you to briefly justify your vote. This justification can involve giving a counterexample (if false) or outlining which definition/theorem the statement follows from (if true).
(a)
We say that a series \(\sum_n a_n\) converges if \(a_n \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\)
(b)
If \(a_n \lt 0\) for all \(n \in \N\) then the series \(\sum_n a_n\) exists.
(c)
The series \(\sum_n a_n\) converges absolutely if and only if \(\sum_n|a_n| \lt \infty\text{.}\)
(d)
If \(\sum_n a_n \in (0, \infty)\) and \(\sum_n b_n \in (-\infty, 0)\) then \(\sum_n (a_n+b_n) = 0\text{.}\)
(e)
If \(a_n \leq 2^{-n}\) for all \(n \in \N\) then \(\sum_n a_n \) converges.
(f)
If \(|a_n| > 2^{-n}\) for all \(n \in \N\) then \(\sum_n a_n \) does not converge absolutely.
(g)
Suppose that \((a_n)_n\) is a sequence of non-negative reals. If \(\sum_n (-1)^{n-1}a_n\) converges then the sequence \((a_n)_n\) is decreasing and converges to zero.
(h)
The series \(\sum_n a_n\) converges if and only if \(a_n\to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\)
(i)
Define the function \(f:(-1, 1) \to \R\) by \(f(x):=\frac{1}{1-x}\) when \(|x|\lt 1\text{.}\) Then this function can be written as a power series.
(j)
The radius of convergence for the power series \(\sum_n 2^n x^n\) is \(1/2\text{.}\)
(k)
In this course, the exponential function is defined as the inverse of the natural logarithm.
(l)
The multiplication rule for series states that if \(\sum_n a_n\) and \(\sum_nb_n\) are absolutely convergent series, then the product is also an absolutely convergent series satisfying the formula
\begin{equation*} \sum_na_n\sum_nb_n = \sum_n a_nb_n\text{.} \end{equation*}
(m)
According to our definition, it is the case that \(x\to 1\) as \(x\to 0\) through the empty set \(\emptyset\text{.}\)
(n)
Define \(f: \R\setminus\set{0} \to \R\) by setting \(f(x):=-1\) when \(x \lt 0\) and \(f(x):=+1\) when \(x \gt 0\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is continuous.
(o)
If \(f: \R \to \R\) is continuous and satisfies \(f(0)\geq 0\text{,}\) then there exists a neighbourhood \([-\eps, \eps]\) of 0, for some \(\eps \gt 0\text{,}\) such that \(f(x)\geq 0\) for all \(x \in [-\eps, \eps]\text{.}\)
