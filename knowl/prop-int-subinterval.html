Proposition 9.20. Restricting to a sub-interval preserves integrability.
Let \(a, b\in \R\) with \(a\lt b\text{.}\) Let \(c, d \in [a,b]\) with \(c \lt d\text{.}\) Let \(f: [a,b] \to \R\) be functions which is Riemann integrable on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is also Riemann integrable on \([c,d]\) and
\begin{equation*} \int_c^d f = \int_a^b f1_{[c,d]}. \end{equation*}
Furthermore, if \(c \in (a,b)\) then we have the interval additivity property
\begin{equation} \int_a^b f = \int_a^c f+ \int_c^b f.\tag{9.3} \end{equation}
Proof.
Since products of Riemann integrable functions are integrable ([cross-reference to target(s) "thm-int-mult" missing or not unique]) and indicators of intervals are integrable (Proposition 9.19), the function \(f1_{(c,d)}\) is Riemann integrable on \([a, b]\text{.}\) By Riemann sum approximation ([cross-reference to target(s) "prop-eps-riemann" missing or not unique]), for any \(\eps \gt 0\) there exists a subdivision \(a = x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = b\) such that
\begin{equation} U(f1_{(c,d)}; x_0, \dots, x_N) - \eps \leq \int_a^bf1_{(c,d)} \leq L(f1_{(c,d)}; x_0, \dots, x_N) + \eps.\tag{9.4} \end{equation}
Since refining a subdivision does not increase the upper sum and does not decrease the lower sum (Proposition 9.8), we may assume that the subdivision \(a = x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = b\) contains \(c\) and \(d\text{.}\) Let \(m \lt n\) be such that \(x_m = c\) and \(x_n = d\text{.}\) Notice that \(c= x_m \lt \dots \lt x_n = d\) is a subdivision of \([c,d]\text{.}\) Moreover
\begin{gather*} U(f1_{(c,d)}; x_0, \dots, x_N) = U(f1_{(c,d)};x_n, \dots, x_m),\\ L(f1_{(c,d)}; x_0, \dots, x_N) = L(f1_{(c,d)};x_n, \dots, x_m). \end{gather*}
Thus for every \(\eps \gt 0\) we have
\begin{equation*} U(f1_{(c,d)}; [c,d])-\eps \leq \int_a^b f1_{(c,d)} \leq L(f1_{(c,d)}; [c,d])+\eps \end{equation*}
Since this holds for every \(\eps \gt 0\text{,}\) we deduce that \(f1_{(c,d)}\) is Riemann integrable on \([c,d]\) with
\begin{equation*} \int_c^df1_{(c,d)} = \int_a^b f1_{(c,d)} \end{equation*}
Since altering a function on a finite set of points does not affect the integral (Proposition 9.18) we have
\begin{equation*} \int_a^b f1_{[c,d]} = \int_a^b f1_{(c,d)} = \int_c^d f1_{(c,d)} = \int_c^d f . \end{equation*}
To deduce (9.3) we use the above, together with linearity of integration (Theorem 9.13) and the fact that indicators of points have zero integral (Proposition 9.18) to yield
\begin{align*} \int_a^b f \amp = \int_a^b f(1_{[a,c)} + 1_{[c,b]}) = \int_a^b f1_{[a,c)} + \int_a^b1_{[c,b]}\\ \amp = \int_a^b f1_{[a,c]} + \int_a^b1_{[c,b]} = \int_a^c f + \int_c^bf. \end{align*}
