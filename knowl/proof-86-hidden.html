The function \(f\circ \phi\) is integrable on any closed interval in \((a,b)\text{,}\) since the composition of continuous functions is continuous (Proposition 6.7). Hence the function \((f\circ \phi)\phi'\) is integrable on any closed interval in \((a,b)\text{,}\) as it is the product of Riemann integrable functions (Theorem 9.13). Define the functions
\begin{equation*} F(s):=\int_{\phi(x)}^{s} f \quad\text{and}\quad G(t) :=\int_x^t \brac{f\circ \phi}\phi'. \end{equation*}
We would like to show that \(F(\phi(y)) = G(y)\text{.}\) By the fundamental theorem of calculus (Theorem 10.2) we have
\begin{equation*} F'(s) = f(s) \quad \text{and} \quad G'(t) = f(\phi(t))\phi'(t). \end{equation*}
By the chain rule (Theorem 7.3) we have
\begin{equation*} (F\circ\phi)'(t) = f(\phi(t))\phi'(t). \end{equation*}
Since functions on an interval with zero derivative are constant (Corollary 7.11) there exists \(C \in \R\) such that for all \(t\in (a,b)\) we have
\begin{equation*} (F\circ\phi)(t)-G(t) = C. \end{equation*}
Since \((F\circ \phi) (x) = 0\) and \(G(x) = 0\text{,}\) we deduce that \(C = 0\text{.}\) The required identity follows.
