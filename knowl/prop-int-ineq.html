Proposition 9.22. Integrability is preserved under taking min, max and absolute values.
Let \(a, b\in \R\) with \(a\lt b\text{.}\) Let \(f,g : [a,b] \to \R\) be functions which are Riemann integrable on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then the following functions are all Riemann integrable on \([a,b]\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \max\set{f,g}, \qquad\min\set{f,g},\qquad f^+,\qquad f^-,\qquad |f|. \end{equation*}
Moreover we have the integral triangle inequality
\begin{equation*} \abs{\int_a^bf}\leq \int_a^b|f|. \end{equation*}
Proof.
We have \(\max\set{f, g} = (f-g)^+ + g\text{.}\) This sum is Riemann integrable by linearity of integration (Theorem 9.17). Since \(\min\set{f,g} = -\max\set{-f,-g}\text{,}\) we deduce that this is also Riemann integrable using the scaling rule (Theorem 9.17). Similarly, we have Riemann integrability of \(f^- = -\max\set{-f, 0}\text{.}\) Finally \(|f| = f^+ + f^-\) is integrable by the previous results and the addition rule (Theorem 9.17). For the triangle inequality, linearity of integration (Theorem 9.17) gives
\begin{equation*} \abs{\int_a^b f} = \abs{\int_a^b f^+ - \int_a^b f^-} \leq \int_a^b f^+ + \int_a^b f^- = \int_a^b\brac{ f^+ + f^-} = \int_a^b\abs{ f} . \end{equation*}
