Recall that the limit \(\sum_n |a_n|\) always exists (but is possibly infinite), by the monotone convergence principle (Proposition 2.3), as in Proposition 4.7. To prove that the series \(\sum_n a_n\) converges, it suffices to prove that the sequence of partial sums \(\sum_{n=1}^N a_n\) is a Cauchy sequence (Definition 3.27), since Cauchy sequences converge (Proposition 3.28). To this end, let \(\eps \gt 0\text{.}\) Since we are assuming that \(\sum_{n=1}^N |a_n|\) tends to a real limit as \(N \to \infty\text{,}\) we know that this is a Cauchy sequence (Proposition 3.28). Hence there exists \(N_0 \in \N\) such that for all \(M\gt N \geq N_0\) we have
\begin{equation*} \eps \geq \abs{\sum_{n=1}^M |a_n| - \sum_{n=1}^N |a_n|} = \sum_{n=N+1}^M |a_n|. \end{equation*}
By the triangle inequality, for all \(M \gt N \geq N_0\) we have that
\begin{equation*} \abs{ \sum_{n=1}^M a_n - \sum_{n=1}^N a_n} = \abs{\sum_{n=N+1}^M a_n} \leq \sum_{n=N+1}^M |a_n| \leq \eps. \end{equation*}
This establishes the Cauchy property, as claimed.
