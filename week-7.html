Section 7 Week 7

Lecture 7.1. Rules for differentiation.

(a)

State and prove both the addition and product rules for differentiation.
Solution.
Proposition 7.2. Algebra of differentiation.
Let \(f,g: (a,b)\to \R\) be differentiable at \(x_0 \in (a,b)\text{.}\) Then the sum \(f+g\) and product \(f\cdot g\) are differentiable at \(x_0\text{.}\) Moreover, their derivatives satisfy the identities
\begin{align*} (f+g)'(x_0) \amp = f'(x_0) + g'(x_0), \amp \text{(addition rule)}\\ (f\cdot g)'(x_0) \amp = f'(x_0)g(x_0) + f(x_0)g'(x_0).\amp \text{(product rule)} \end{align*}
Proof.
We verify both identities using the sequential characterisation of differentiability (Proposition 6.23) together with the addition and product rules for sequence limits (Proposition 2.8 and Proposition 2.10). To this end, let \((a_n)_n\) be a sequence in \((a,b)\setminus\set{x_0}\) with \(a_n \to x_0\text{.}\) Then the addition rule follows from the addition rule for sequence limits, since
\begin{align*} \lim_{n \to \infty} \amp \frac{(f+g)(a_n)-(f+g)(x_0)}{a_n-x_0}\\ \amp = \lim_{n\to \infty} \frac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}{a_n-x_0}+ \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)}{x-x_0}. \end{align*}
The product rule uses the identity
\begin{align*} \amp f(a_n)g(a_n) - f(x_0)g(x_0) = \sqbrac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}g(a_n) + f(x_0)\sqbrac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)}\\ \amp =\sqbrac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}\sqbrac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)} + \sqbrac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}g(x_0)+ f(x_0)\sqbrac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)}. \end{align*}
By the multiplication rule for sequence limits (Proposition 2.10), we have
\begin{gather*} \lim_{n \to \infty}\frac{\sqbrac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}g(x_0)}{a_n-x_0} = f'(x_0)g(x_0),\\ \lim_{n \to \infty}\frac{f(x_0)\sqbrac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)}}{a_n-x_0} = f(x_0)g'(x_0). \end{gather*}
Similarly
\begin{align*} \amp \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\sqbrac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}\sqbrac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)}}{a_n-x_0}\\ \amp = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\sqbrac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}}{a_n-x_0}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\sqbrac{g(a_n)-g(x_0)}}{a_n-x_0}\lim_{n\to\infty}(a_n-x_0)\\ \amp = f'(x_0)\cdot g'(x_0)\cdot 0 = 0. \end{align*}

(b)

State and prove the chain rule for differentiation.
Solution.
Theorem 7.3. Chain rule.
Let \(f : (a,b) \to (c,d)\) and \(g: (c,d) \to \R\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f\) is differentiable at \(x_0\) and that \(g\) is differentiable at \(f(x_0)\text{.}\) Then the composition \(g\circ f\) is differentiable at \(x_0\text{.}\) Moreover the derivative satisfies the identity
\begin{equation*} (g\circ f)'(x_0) = g'\bigbrac{f(x_0)} \cdot f'(x_0). \end{equation*}
Proof.
Let \((x_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be a sequence in \((a,b) \setminus\set{x_0}\) with \(x_n \to x_0\text{.}\) By Proposition 6.23, we wish to prove that
\begin{equation} \lim_{n\to \infty} \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{x_n-x_0} = g'\bigbrac{f(x_0)}f'(x_0).\tag{7.1} \end{equation}
We have
\begin{equation} \frac{g\bigbrac{f( x_n)} - g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{ x_n-x_0} = \begin{cases} 0, \amp \text{if } f( x_n) = f(x_0);\\ \frac{g\brac{f( x_n)}-g\brac{f(x_0)}}{f( x_n)-f(x_0)}\cdot \frac{f( x_n)-f(x_0)}{ x_n-x_0}, \amp \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}\tag{7.2} \end{equation}

First, let us suppose that \(f(x_n) = f(x_0)\) for only finitely many values of \(n\text{.}\) Then there exists \(N_0\in \N\) such that for all \(n \geq N_0\) we have \(f(x_n)\neq f(x_0)\text{.}\) Hence for all \(n \geq N_0\) we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{x_n-x_0} = \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}\cdot \frac{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}{x_n-x_0} . \end{equation*}

Since differentiability implies continuity (Proposition 6.24), we have

\begin{equation*} \lim_{n \to \infty} f(x_n) = f(x_0). \end{equation*}

Thus by the sequential characterisation of differentiability (Proposition 6.23) we have

\begin{equation} \lim_{\substack{n\to \infty\\ n \geq N_0}} \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{f(x_n)-f(x_0)} = g'\bigbrac{f(x_0)}.\tag{7.3} \end{equation}

We may therefore use the multiplication rule for sequential limits (Proposition 2.10) to deduce that

\begin{align*} \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{x_n-x_0} \amp = \lim_{\substack{n\to \infty\\ n \geq N_0}}\brac{ \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}\cdot \frac{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}{x_n-x_0}}\\ \amp = \lim_{\substack{n\to \infty\\ n \geq N_0}}\frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_n)}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}\lim_{n\to \infty} \frac{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}{x_n-x_0}\\ \amp = g'\bigbrac{f(x_0)}f'(x_0). \end{align*}

Next, let us suppose that \(f(x_n) = f(x_0)\) for infinitely many values of \(n\text{.}\) Write \(k_1 \lt k_2 \lt \dots\) for those indices for which \(f(x_{k_n}) = f(x_0)\text{.}\) Write \(\ell_1 \lt \ell_2 \lt \dots\) for the (possibly finite) remaining indices with \(f(x_{l_n}) \neq f(x_0)\text{.}\) The sequence

\begin{equation} n\mapsto \frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_{\ell_n})}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{f(x_{\ell_n})-f(x_0)}\tag{7.4} \end{equation}

is either a finite sequence, or converges as in the proof of (7.3). In either case, the sequence is bounded, so there exists \(M \in \R\) such that for all \(\ell_n\) we have

\begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{g\bigbrac{f(x_{\ell_n})}-g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{f(x_{\ell_n})-f(x_0)} } \leq M. \end{equation*}

Combining this with (7.2), for any \(n \in \N\) we have

\begin{equation} \abs{\frac{g\bigbrac{f( x_n)} - g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{ x_n-x_0}} \leq M\abs{\frac{f(x_{n}) - f(x_0)}{x_{n} - x_0}}.\tag{7.5} \end{equation}

Since \(x_{k_n} \to x_0\) we have

\begin{equation*} f'(x_0) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{f(x_{k_n}) - f(x_0)}{x_{k_n} - x_0} = 0. \end{equation*}

So it follows that

\begin{equation*} \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{f(x_{n}) - f(x_0)}{x_{n} - x_0} = f'(x_0) = 0. \end{equation*}

Combining this with (7.5) gives

\begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to \infty}\abs{\frac{g\bigbrac{f( x_n)} - g\bigbrac{f(x_0)}}{ x_n-x_0}} = 0. \end{equation*}

Hence (7.1) also follows in this case.

(c)

State and prove the quotient rule for differentiation.
Solution.
Corollary 7.4. Quotient rule.
Let \(f,g : (a,b) \to \R\) both be differentiable at \(x_0 \in D\text{.}\) Suppose that \(g(x_0) \neq 0\text{.}\) Then the quotient \(f/g\) is defined on \((x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)\) for some \(\delta \gt 0\) and is differentiable at \(x_0\text{.}\) Moreover the derivative satisfies the identity
\begin{equation} (f/g)'(x_0) = \frac{f'(x_0)g(x_0)-f(x_0)g'(x_0)}{g(x_0)^2}.\tag{7.6} \end{equation}
Proof.
Since differentiability implies continuity (Proposition 6.24), the function \(g\) is continuous at \(x_0\text{.}\) Since \(g(x_0) \neq 0\text{,}\) and non-zero points of continuity yield a non-zero neighbourhood (Proposition 5.20), there exists \(\delta \gt0\) such that \(g(x) \neq 0\) for all \(x \in (a,b)\cap (x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)\text{.}\) Decreasing \(\delta\) if necessary, we may assume that \((x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)\subset (a,b)\text{.}\) Hence the quotient \(x \mapsto f(x)/g(x)\) is defined on \((x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)\text{.}\) Composing the reciprocal function \(y \mapsto 1/y\) with \(g\text{,}\) the chain rule (Theorem 7.3) and Example 6.27 together give that the function
\begin{equation*} x\mapsto \recip{g(x)} \end{equation*}
is differentiable at \(x_0\text{.}\) Moreover, the derivative satisfies
\begin{equation*} \brac{\recip{g(x_0)}}' = -\frac{g'(x_0)}{g(x_0)^2}. \end{equation*}
Hence by the product rule (Proposition 7.2) the quotient \(f/g = f \cdot\brac{1/g}\) is differentiable at \(x_0\) with derivative
\begin{equation*} \frac{f'(x_0)}{g(x_0)} - \frac{f(x_0)g'(x_0)}{g(x_0)^2}. \end{equation*}
This completes the proof of the quotient rule.

(d)

Using the above, justify why rational functions are differentiable.
Solution.
Example 7.5. Polynomials and rational functions are differentiable.
Since the identity function \(x \mapsto x\) is differentiable on \(\R\text{,}\) the product rule gives that \(x \mapsto x^2\) is differentiable on \(\R\text{.}\) Using induction and the product rule, it follows that the for any \(n \in \N\) the function \(x \mapsto x^n = x\cdot x^{n-1}\) is differentiable on \(\R\) with derivative
\begin{equation*} (x^n)' = nx^{n-1}. \end{equation*}
Since constant functions \(x \mapsto c\) are differentiable on \(\R\text{,}\) with zero derivative, it follows from the product rule that the function \(x \mapsto c x^n\) is differentiable on \(\R\) with derivative
\begin{equation*} (cx^n)' = nc x^{n-1}. \end{equation*}
By the addition rule for differentiation (Proposition 7.2) it follows that any polynomial
\begin{equation*} x \mapsto c_0 + c_1 x + \dots + c_n x^n \end{equation*}
is differentiable on \(\R\text{,}\) with derivative
\begin{equation*} c_1 + 2c_2x + \dots + n c_n x^{n-1}. \end{equation*}
By the quotient rule for differentiation (Corollary 7.4) we deduce that any rational function (see Definition 6.5) is differentiable on its domain of definition.

Lecture 7.6. The mean value theorem.

(a)

Define a local max/min.
Solution.
Definition 7.7. Local max/min.
Let \(f : (a,b) \to \R\text{.}\) We say that \(x_0 \in (a,b)\) is a local maximum for \(f\) if there exists \(\delta \gt 0\) such that
\begin{equation*} (\forall x \in (x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)) [f(x) \leq f(x_0)]. \end{equation*}
We say that \(x_0 \in (a,b)\) is a local minimum for \(f\) if there exists \(\delta \gt 0\) such that
\begin{equation*} (\forall x \in (x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)) [f(x) \geq f(x_0)]. \end{equation*}

(b)

State and prove how a derivative must be zero at a local max/min.
Solution.
Proposition 7.8. The derivative is zero at a local max/min.
Suppose that \(f : (a,b) \to \R\) is differentiable at \(x_0 \in (a,b)\text{.}\) Suppose that \(x_0\) is a local maximum or local minimum for \(f\text{.}\) Then \(f'(x_0) = 0\text{.}\)
Proof.
Let us suppose that \(x_0\) is a local maximum for \(f\text{;}\) the argument for the local minimum is similar. By definition of a local maximum (see Definition 7.7) there exists \(\delta \gt 0\) such that for all \(x \in (x_0-\delta, x_0+\delta)\) we have \(f(x_0) \geq f(x)\text{.}\) Hence
\begin{equation*} \frac{f(x)-f(x_0)}{x-x_0} \begin{cases} \geq 0 \amp \text{ if } x_0-\delta \lt x \lt x_0,\\ \leq 0 \amp \text{ if } x_0 \lt x \lt x_0+\delta.\end{cases} \end{equation*}
Setting \(a_n := x_0-\trecip{n+1}\delta\text{,}\) the sequential characterisation of the derivative (Proposition 6.23) and the inequality rule (Proposition 2.2) give that
\begin{equation*} f'(x_0) = \lim_{n\to \infty}\frac{f(a_n)-f(x_0)}{a_n-x_0} \geq 0. \end{equation*}
Similarly, setting \(b_0:= x_0+\trecip{n+1}\delta\) gives
\begin{equation*} f'(x_0) = \lim_{n\to \infty}\frac{f(b_n)-f(x_0)}{a_n-x_0} \leq 0. \end{equation*}
Thus \(f'(x_0) = 0\text{.}\)

(c)

State and prove Rolle's theorem.
Solution.
Theorem 7.9. Rolle's theorem.
Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be a continuous function which is differentiable on \((a,b)\text{,}\) with \(a \lt b\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f(a) = f(b)\text{.}\) Then there exists \(x_0 \in (a,b)\) such that \(f'(x_0) = 0\text{.}\)
Proof.
By the extreme value theorem (Theorem 6.20), there exists \(x_1,x_2 \in [a,b]\) which are minima and maxima (respectively) for \(f\) on \([a,b]\text{.}\) In particular \(x_1\) is a local maximum and \(x_2\) is a local minimum. If \(x_1 \in (a,b)\) or \(x_2 \in (a,b)\text{,}\) then it follows that \(f'(x_1) = 0\) or \(f'(x_2) = 0\) since local minima/maxima have zero derivative at points of differentiability (Proposition 7.8). We may therefore assume that \(\set{x_1, x_2} \subset \set{a,b}\text{.}\) Hence \(f(x_1) = f(x_2)\text{.}\) For any \(x \in [a,b]\text{,}\) we have that \(f(x)\) is between \(f(x_1)\) and \(f(x_2)\text{.}\) Since \(f(x_1) = f(x_2)\text{,}\) it follows that \(f\) is constant on \([a,b]\text{.}\) It then follows from Example 6.25 that \(f'(x)= 0\) at every \(x \in (a,b)\text{,}\) e.g. for \(x := \frac{a+b}{2}\text{.}\)

(d)

State and prove the mean value theorem.
Solution.
Theorem 7.10. Mean value theorem.
Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be a continuous function which is differentiable on \((a,b)\text{,}\) with \(a \lt b\text{.}\) Then there exists \(x_0 \in (a,b)\) such that
\begin{equation} f(b) - f(a) = f'(x_0)(b-a).\tag{7.7} \end{equation}
Proof.
We deduce this result from Rolle's theorem (Theorem 7.9). The idea of the proof is to try and find a continuous function \(g:[a,b] \to \R\) which is differentiable on \((a,b)\) with \(g(a) = g(b)\) and such that the identity \(g'(x_0) = 0\) implies (7.7). To this end set
\begin{equation*} g(x) :=f(x) - \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} (x-a), \qquad (x \in [a,b]). \end{equation*}
This is continuous on \([a,b]\) since constant functions are continuous (Example 5.14), the identity function is continuous (Example 5.15) and so is the sum/product of continuous functions (Proposition 6.2). The function \(g\) is differentiable on \((a,b)\) since constant functions are differentiable (Example 6.25), the identity function is differentiable (Example 6.26) and so is the sum/product of differentiable functions (Proposition 6.2). We have \(g(a) = f(a)\) and \(g(b) = f(a)\text{.}\) We may therefore apply Rolle's theorem to deduce the existence of \(x_0 \in (a,b)\) such that \(g'(x_0) = 0\text{.}\) Employing the sum/product rules for differentiation, we have that
\begin{equation*} g'(x_0) = f'(x_0) -\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}. \end{equation*}
The result follows on re-arranging the identity
\begin{equation*} 0 = f'(x_0) -\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}. \end{equation*}

(e)

Prove how a function with non-negative derivative is increasing.
Solution.
Corollary 7.11.
Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be a continuous function which is differentiable on \((a,b)\text{,}\) with \(a \lt b\text{.}\)

  1. If \((\forall x \in (a,b))[f'(x) \geq 0]\) then \(f\) is increasing.

  2. If \((\forall x \in (a,b))[f'(x) \gt 0]\) then \(f\) is strictly increasing.

  3. If \((\forall x \in (a,b))[f'(x) \leq 0]\) then \(f\) is decreasing.

  4. If \((\forall x \in (a,b))[f'(x) \lt 0]\) then \(f\) is strictly decreasing.

  5. If \((\forall x \in (a,b))[f'(x) = 0]\) then \(f\) is constant.

Proof.
Let us first suppose that \((\forall x \in (a,b))[f'(x) \geq 0]\text{.}\) Let \(x_1, x_2 \in [a,b]\) with \(x_1 \lt x_2\text{.}\) Our task is to prove that \(f(x_1) \leq f(x_2)\text{.}\) Applying the mean value theorem to the function \(f: [x_1, x_2] \to \R\text{,}\) there exists \(x_0 \in (x_1, x_2)\) such that
\begin{equation*} f(x_2)-f(x_1) = f'(x_0)(x_2-x_1) \geq 0, \end{equation*}
as required. Notice that if \((\forall x \in (a,b))[f'(x) \gt 0]\) then \(f(x_2) -f(x_1) = f'(x_0)(x_2-x_1) \gt 0\text{,}\) which implies that the function is strictly increasing. The conclusions involving decreasing functions follow on applying the increasing results to \(-f\text{.}\) If \((\forall x \in (a,b))[f'(x) = 0]\) then \(f\) is both increasing and decreasing, hence for all \(x \in [a,b]\) we have \(f(x) = f(b)\text{.}\) Thus in the latter case \(f\) is constant.

Lecture 7.12. Taylor's theorem.

(a)

State and prove Taylor's theorem.
Solution.
Theorem 7.13.
Let \(f : (a,b) \to \R\) be a function which is \(n\)-times differentiable and let \(x_0, x \in (a,b)\text{.}\) Then there exists \(x_n\) between \(x_0\) and \(x\) such that
\begin{align} \amp \abs{f(x) - \sqbrac{f(x_0) + (x-x_0)f'(x_0) + \dots + \trecip{(n-1)!} (x-x_0)^{n-1} f^{(n-1)}(x_0)}}\notag\\ \amp \qquad\qquad\qquad \leq |x-x_0|^n|f^{(n)}(x_n)|.\tag{7.8} \end{align}
Proof.
Set
\begin{align*} \amp g(x) := f(x) -\\ \amp \sqbrac{f(x_0) + (x-x_0)f'(x_0) + \trecip{2} (x-x_0)^2 f''(x_0) + \dots + \trecip{(n-1)!} (x-x_0)^{n-1} f^{(n-1)}(x_0)}\text{.} \end{align*}
Our aim is to prove that there exists \(x_n\) between \(x_0\) and \(x\) such that
\begin{equation} \abs{g(x)} \leq |x-x_0|^n \abs{g^{(n)}(x_n)}.\tag{7.9} \end{equation}
We do this by induction on \(n \geq 0\text{,}\) the base case \(n=0\) being automatic. Suppose then that (7.9) holds for \(n-1\text{,}\) so that there exists \(x_{n-1}\) between \(x_0\) and \(x\) such that
\begin{equation*} \abs{g(x)} \leq |x-x_0|^{n-1} \abs{g^{(n-1)}(x_{n-1})}. \end{equation*}
Applying the mean value theorem to \(g^{(n-1)}\text{,}\) there exists \(x_{n}\) between \(x_0\) and \(x_{n-1}\) such that
\begin{equation*} g^{(n-1)}(x_{n-1}) = g^{(n-1)}(x_{0}) + (x_{n-1}-x_0) g^{(n)}(x_n) = (x_{n-1}-x_0) g^{(n)}(x_n). \end{equation*}
Since \(x_{n-1}\) is between \(x_0\) and \(x\text{,}\) we must have that \(x_{n}\) is between \(x_0\) and \(x\) and
\begin{equation*} |x_{n-1}-x_0|\leq |x-x_0|\text{.} \end{equation*}
Therefore
\begin{equation*} \abs{g(x)} \leq |x-x_0|^{n-1} \abs{(x_{n-1}-x_0) g^{(n)}(x_n)} \leq |x-x_0|^{n} \abs{g^{(n)}(x_n)}, \end{equation*}
as required.
Remark 7.14. Taylor with improved remainder.
With a slightly longer proof, one can actually improve our statement of Taylor's theorem to the following.
Theorem 7.15. Strong Taylor (non-examinable).
Let \(f : (a,b) \to \R\) be a function which is \(n\)-times differentiable and let \(x_0, x \in (a,b)\text{.}\) Then there exists \(x_n\) between \(x_0\) and \(x\) such that
\begin{align*} f(x) = f(x_0) + (x-x_0)f'(x_0) + \dots + \amp \trecip{(n-1)!} (x-x_0)^{n-1} f^{(n-1)}(x_0)\\ \amp + \trecip{n!}(x-x_0)^nf^{(n)}(x_n). \end{align*}

Lecture 7.16. The inverse function theorem.

(a)

State and prove how differentiable functions with continuous inverse have a differentiable inverse.
Solution.
Theorem 7.17. Inverse function theorem.
Let \(f: (a,b) \to (c,d)\) be a bijective function and let \(x_0 \in (a,b)\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f^{-1}\) is continuous at \(y_0:=f(x_0)\) and that \(f\) is differentiable at \(x_0\) with
\begin{equation*} f'(x_0) \neq 0. \end{equation*}
Then \(f^{-1}\) is differentiable at \(y_0\) with derivative
\begin{equation} \brac{f^{-1}}'(y_0) = \recip{f'(x_0)}.\tag{7.10} \end{equation}
Proof.
By the sequential characterisation of differentiability (Proposition 6.23), we are required to prove that if \((y_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) is a sequence in \((c,d)\setminus\set{y_0}\) with \(y_n \to y_0\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation*} \frac{f^{-1}(y_n) - f^{-1}(y_0)}{y_n-y_0} \to \recip{f'(x_0)}. \end{equation*}
Since \(f\) is bijective, for each \(y_n\in (c,d)\setminus\set{y_0}\) there exists \(x_n \in (a,b)\setminus\set{x_0}\) such that \(f(x_n) = y_n\text{.}\) Thus \((x_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) is a sequence in \((a,b)\setminus\set{x_0}\text{.}\) Since \(f^{-1}\) is continuous at \(y_0\) we have
\begin{equation*} x_n = f^{-1}(y_n) \to f^{-1}(y_0) = x_0. \end{equation*}
Since \(f\) is differentiable at \(x_0\text{,}\) it follows that
\begin{equation*} \frac{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}{x_n-x_0} \to f'(x_0). \end{equation*}
Since \(f'(x_0) \neq 0\text{,}\) it follows from the reciprocal rule for limits (Proposition 2.12) that
\begin{equation*} \frac{f^{-1}(y_n)-f^{-1}(y_0)}{y_n-y_0} = \frac{x_n-x_0}{f(x_n)-f(x_0)}\to \recip{f'(x_0)}. \end{equation*}

Lecture 7.18. Differentiability of power series.

(a)

State and prove how power series are infinitely differentiable within their radius of convergence.
Solution.
Theorem 7.19. Power series are infinitely differentiable within radius of convergence.
Let \((a_n)_{n=0}^\infty\) be a sequence of real numbers. Let \(R \in [0, \infty]\) denote the radius of convergence (see Theorem 5.5) of the corresponding power series:
\begin{equation} f(x) := \sum_{n =0}^\infty a_n x^n.\tag{7.11} \end{equation}
Then for any \(m \in \Z_{\geq0}\) the function \(f : (-R, R) \to \R\) given by (7.11) is \(m\)-times differentiable. Moreover, for any \(x_0 \in (-R, R)\) we have the formula
\begin{equation} f^{(m)}(x_0) = \sum_{n = m}^\infty n(n-1)\dotsm (n-m+1)a_n x_0^{n-m}\tag{7.12} \end{equation}
where the right-hand side of (7.12) is absolutely convergent.
Proof.

We prove the result for \(m=1\) and note that the general case then follows by re-applying the \(m=1\) case \(m\) times.

In proving the \(m=1\) case, let us first show that the right-hand side of (7.12) is absolutely convergent. Let \(r\) be a real number satisfying \(|x_0| \lt r \lt R\text{.}\) Such a number exists since \(|x_0| \lt R\text{,}\) for instance we could set

\begin{equation*} r:=\frac{|x_0|+R}{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We claim that the following infinite series is convergent

\begin{equation} \sum_{n=2}^\infty n^2 |a_n| r^{n-2}.\tag{7.13} \end{equation}

To see this, let \(r_1\) be a real satisfying \(r \lt r_1 \lt R\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=2}^\infty n^2 |a_n| r^{n-2} \leq r^{-2}\sum_{n=2}^\infty n^2 (r/r_1)^n |a_n| r_1^n. \end{equation*}

Since exponentials beat polynomials (Proposition 3.7), we have that \(n^2 (r/r_1)^n \to 0\text{,}\) in particular the sequence \(n^2 (r/r_1)^n\) is bounded. It follows from the comparison test (Proposition 4.9) and absolute convergence of (7.11) that (7.13) converges. Again by the comparison test (Proposition 4.9), this implies absolute convergence of

\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty n a_n x^{n-1}. \end{equation*}

With the above facts on absolute convergence established, we are able to prove the \(m=1\) case of (7.12). By linearity of series (Proposition 4.5), for \(x \in [-r,r]\) with \(x\neq x_0\) we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{f(x) - f(x_0)}{x-x_0} - \sum_{n=1}^\infty na_n x_0^{n-1} = \recip{x-x_0}\sum_{n=2}^\infty a_n \sqbrac{x^n-x_0^n-(x-x_0)nx_0^{n-1}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

By Taylor's theorem (Theorem 7.13), for each \(n\in \N\) there exists \(x_n\) between \(x_0\) and \(x\) such that

\begin{equation*} \abs{x^n-x_0^n-(x-x_0)nx_0^{n-1}} \leq n(n-1)|x-x_0|^2|x_n|^{n-2}\leq n^2|x-x_0|^2r^{n-2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Therefore

\begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{f(x) - f(x_0)}{x-x_0} - \sum_{n=1}^\infty na_n x_0^{n-1}} \leq \abs{x-x_0}\sum_{n=2}^\infty n^2|a_n|r^{n-2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

It follows that Definition 6.22 is satisfied on taking

\begin{equation*} \delta(x_0, \eps) := \min\set{\frac{\eps}{\sum_{n=2}^\infty n^2|a_n|r^{n-2}}, r-|x_0|} \gt 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
