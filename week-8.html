Section 8 Week 8

Lecture 8.1. The exponential function.

(a)

Prove that the exponential function is infinitely differentiable and calculate its derivative.
Solution.
Proposition 8.2.
The exponential function \(\exp : \R \to \R\text{,}\) given by
\begin{equation*} \exp(x) := \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!} \end{equation*}
is infinitely differentiable with first derivative
\begin{equation} \exp'(x) = \exp(x).\tag{8.1} \end{equation}
Proof.
The infinite radius of convergence of the power series is proved in Proposition 5.8. Infinite differentiability and the formula (8.1) then both follow from Theorem 7.19.

(b)

State and prove a formula for the reciprocal of the exponential function.
Solution.
Proposition 8.3.
For all \(x \in \R\) we have the formula
\begin{equation*} \exp(-x) = \recip{\exp(x)}. \end{equation*}
Proof.
The addition law \(\exp(x+y) = \exp(x)\exp(y)\) is established in Proposition 5.9. From the addition law, it follows that
\begin{equation*} \exp(x)\exp(-x) = \exp(0) = 1. \end{equation*}
Thus \(\exp(-x) = 1/\exp(x)\text{.}\)

(c)

Justify why the exponential function is positive everywhere.
Solution.
By the inequality rule for sequences (Proposition 2.2), when \(x \geq 0\) we have
\begin{equation*} \exp(x) = \lim_{N\to \infty} \brac{1 + x + \trecip{2} x^2 + \dots + \trecip{N!} x^N} \geq 1 + x. \end{equation*}
Hence \(\exp(x) \gt 0\) when \(x \geq 0\text{.}\) Again if \(x \geq 0\) then \(\exp(-x) = \recip{\exp(x)} \gt0\text{.}\) Thus for all \(x \in \R\) we have \(\exp(x) \gt 0\text{.}\)

(d)

State and prove the limit of the exponential function as its argument tends to infinity.
Solution.
\(\exp(x) \to \infty\) as \(x\to \infty\text{.}\)
Proof.
By the definition of function limits (Definition 5.11), we are required to show that if \(a_n\to \infty\) then \(\exp(a_n)\to \infty\text{.}\) We note that by definition of tending to infinity (Definition 1.17) there exists \(N\in \N\) such that for all \(n\geq N\) we have \(a_n\geq 0\text{.}\) For \(n\geq N\) we therefore have the inequality
\begin{equation*} \exp(a_n) = 1+a_n+\trecip{2} a_n^2 + \dots \geq a_n . \end{equation*}
Since \(a_n\to \infty\) the one-sided sandwich rule for sequence limits (Proposition 2.5) gives that \(\exp(a_n)\to \infty\text{.}\)

(e)

Justify why the exponential function is strictly increasing.
Solution.
Since \(\exp'(x) = \exp(x) \gt0\) and functions with positive derivative are strictly increasing (Corollary 7.11), we deduce that \(\exp\) is strictly increasing on \(\R\text{.}\)

(f)

State and prove how the exponential function is a bijection onto its image.
Solution.
Proposition 8.4.
\(\exp\) is a bijection from \(\R\) to \((0, \infty)\text{.}\)
Proof.
Strictly increasing functions are injective, hence to show that \(\exp\) is bijective \(\R \to (0, \infty)\text{,}\) we must establish that every \(y\gt 0\) can be represented as \(y = \exp(x)\) for some \(x \in \R\text{.}\) Since \(\exp(x) \to \infty\) as \(x \to \infty\text{,}\) we have \(\exp(-x) = 1/\exp(x) \to 0\) as \(x \to \infty\text{.}\) Thus, for any \(y \in (0, \infty)\text{,}\) there exists \(x_1 \lt x_2\) such that
\begin{equation*} \exp(x_1) \leq y\leq \exp(x_2). \end{equation*}
Since differentiable functions are continuous, we may apply the intermediate value theorem to deduce the existence of \(x \in [x_1, x_2]\) such that \(\exp(x) = y\text{.}\)

Lecture 8.5. The logarithm function.

(a)

Define the logarithm function and justify why this definition is valid.
Solution.
Definition 8.6. Logarithm.
Since the exponential function \(\exp : \R \to (0, \infty)\) is bijective (Proposition 8.4), it has a well-defined inverse \((0, \infty) \to \R\) which we denote by \(\log x\) for \(x \in (0, \infty)\text{.}\)

(b)

State and prove how the logarithm is a strictly increasing function, which is bijective onto its image.
Solution.
Proposition 8.7.
\(\log\) is a strictly increasing bijection from \((0,\infty)\) to \(\R\text{.}\)
Proof.

The fact that \(\log\) is a bijection from \((0,\infty)\) to \(\R\) follows from its definition as the inverse of the bijection \(\exp : \R \to (0,\infty)\text{.}\)

To prove that it is strictly increasing, we argue by contradiction. Let \(x_1, x_2 \in (0, \infty)\) with \(x_1 \lt x_2\) and suppose that \(\log(x_1) \geq \log(x_2)\text{.}\) The increasing nature of the exponential function (Proposition 8.2) gives that

\begin{equation*} x_1 = \exp\brac{\log x_1} \geq \exp\brac{\log x_2} = x_2. \end{equation*}

This is a contradiction, hence the logarithm is strictly increasing.

(c)

State and prove how the logarithm function is continuous.
Solution.
Proposition 8.8.
\(\log\) is continuous on \((0,\infty)\text{.}\)
Proof.
Let \(x_0\in (0, \infty)\text{.}\) Using the epsilon-delta characterisation of continuity (Proposition 5.19), we are required to prove that for each \(\eps \gt 0\) there exists \(\delta = \delta(x_0,\eps) \gt 0\) such that
\begin{equation*} |x-x_0| \leq \delta \quad \implies \quad |\log(x) - \log(x_0)| \leq \eps. \end{equation*}
Writing \(y:=\log(x)\) and \(y_0:=\log(x_0)\) this is equivalent to showing that
\begin{equation} |\exp(y)-\exp(y_0)| \leq \delta \quad \implies \quad |y - y_0| \leq \eps.\tag{8.2} \end{equation}
By the mean value theorem (Theorem 7.10), there exists \(y_1\) between \(y\) and \(y_0\) such that
\begin{equation} |\exp(y)-\exp(y_0)| = \exp(y_1)|y-y_0|. \tag{8.3} \end{equation}
Since \(\exp\) is increasing, we have
\begin{equation*} \exp(y_1) \geq \exp\brac{\min\set{y_0,y}} = \min\set{x_0,x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Hence we derive (8.2) on taking
\begin{equation*} \delta(x_0,\eps) :=\min\set{\frac{x_0}{2}, \frac{\eps x_0}{2}}, \end{equation*}
since with this choice we have
\begin{equation*} \exp(y_1) \geq \min\set{x_0,x_0-\tfrac{x_0}{2}}=\tfrac{x_0}{2}\text{,} \end{equation*}
which yields the required implication on substitution into (8.3).

(d)

State and prove how the logarithm function is differentiable.
Solution.
Proposition 8.9.
\(\log\) is differentiable on \((0,\infty)\) with derivative
\begin{equation*} \log'(x) = \recip{x}. \end{equation*}
Proof.
This follows from the inverse function theorem (Theorem 7.17) with \(f := \exp\text{,}\) since the hypotheses of that theorem are met, namely:

  • \(\exp : \R \to (0,\infty)\) is bijective (this follows from Proposition 8.4).

  • \(\exp^{-1}= \log\) is continuous at every \(x_0 \in (0,\infty)\) (this follows from Proposition 8.8).

  • \(\exp\) is differentiable at every \(x_0\in \R\) with non-zero derivative (this follows from Proposition 8.2).

(e)

State and prove the limit of the logarithm function as its argument tends to infinity.
Solution.
Proposition 8.10.
\(\log x \to \infty\) as \(x\to\infty\text{.}\)
Proof.
By the definition of function limits (Definition 5.11), we are required to show that if \(a_n\to \infty\) then \(\log(a_n)\to \infty\text{.}\) Let \(M \gt 0\text{.}\) We note that by definition of tending to infinity (Definition 1.17) there exists \(N\in \N\) such that for all \(n\geq N\) we have \(a_n\geq \exp M\text{.}\) Since \(\log\) is increasing this gives \(\log a_n \geq M\) for \(n\geq N\text{.}\) It follows that \(\log(a_n)\to \infty\) by the sequential definition of tending to infinity (Definition 1.17).

Lecture 8.11. Exponentiation to an arbitrary real.

(a)

Prove the identities:
\begin{gather} (\forall a \in (0,\infty))(\forall n \in \Z)[a^n = \exp(n\log a)].\tag{8.4}\\ (\forall a \in (0,\infty))(\forall n \in \N)[\sqrt[n]{a} = \exp\brac{\trecip{n}\log a}].\tag{8.5} \end{gather}
Solution.
By our conventions for integer powers, if \(a \in (0,\infty)\) and \(n \in \Z\) then the addition rule \(\exp(x)\exp(y) = \exp(x+y)\) gives \(\exp(x)^n = \exp(nx)\text{,}\) hence
\begin{equation*} a^n = \brac{\exp(\log a)}^n = \exp(n\log a). \end{equation*}
We observe that \(\exp\brac{\recip{n} \log a}^n = \exp(\log a) = a\text{.}\) Hence \(\exp\brac{\recip{n} \log a}\) is a positive \(n\)th root of \(a\text{.}\) By uniqueness of positive roots (Corollary 6.17) we have \(\sqrt[n]{a} = \exp\brac{\recip{n} \log a}\text{.}\)

(b)

Define exponentiation to an arbitrary real and the logarithm to an arbitrary base.
Solution.
Definition 8.12. Exponentiation to an arbitrary real.
Given \(a \in (0, \infty)\) and \(x \in \R\) we define
\begin{equation*} a^x := \exp(x\log a). \end{equation*}
We have previously defined \(a^n\) when \(n \in \Z\text{,}\) and (8.4) shows that our two definitions coincide when \(x = n \in \Z\text{.}\) (8.5) also shows that \(a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a} \text{,}\) as we would expect.
Definition 8.13. Logarithm to an arbitrary base.
Given \(a \in (0, \infty)\) with \(a \neq 1\) and \(x \in \R\) we define
\begin{equation*} \log_a(x) := \frac{\log x}{\log a}. \end{equation*}

(c)

Justify why \(a^x = b\) if and only if \(\log_a b = x\text{.}\)
Solution.
Since \(\log\) is injective on \((0,\infty)\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} a^x = b \iff \log(a^x) = \log(b). \end{equation*}
By definition we have \(a^x := \exp(x\log a)\text{.}\) Since \(\log = \exp^{-1}\) we deduce that
\begin{equation*} a^x = b \iff x\log a = \log b. \end{equation*}
Since \(a \neq 1\text{,}\) we have \(\log a\neq0\text{,}\) thus we may divide by \(\log a\) to conclude that
\begin{equation*} a^x = b \iff x = \frac{\log b}{\log a}. \end{equation*}
By definition \(\log_a b = \log b / \log a\text{,}\) establishing the claim.

(d)

For \(a >0\) and \(x,y \in \R\text{,}\) prove the identities
\begin{gather*} \log(a^x) = x\log a,\\ \brac{a^x}^y = a^{x y}. \end{gather*}
Solution.

By definition of exponentiation and the fact that \(\log = \exp^{-1}\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \log(a^x) = \log\brac{\exp(x\log a)} = x \log a. \end{equation*}

This establishes the first identity.

Taking logarithms, the identity \(\brac{a^x}^y = a^{x y}\) is equivalent to

\begin{equation*} \log\brac{\brac{a^x}^y} = \log\brac{a^{x y}}. \end{equation*}

This holds on applying our first identity to both sides (twice on the left-hand side).

Lecture 8.14. Definition of the Riemann integral.

(a)

Define a subdivision of a closed interval.
Solution.
Definition 8.15. Subdivision.
Let \(a,b\in \R\) with \(a\lt b\text{.}\) A subdivision of the interval \([a,b]\) is a strictly increasing finite sequence of the form
\begin{equation*} a= x_0 \lt x_1 \lt \dots \lt x_N = b \end{equation*}
for some \(N \in \N\text{.}\) We sometimes denote such a subdivision by \((x_n)_{n=0}^N = (x_0, x_1, \dots, x_N)\text{.}\)

(b)

Define the upper/lower Riemann sums of a function with respect to a subdivision.
Solution.
Definition 8.16. Riemann sum.
Let \(a, b\in \R\) with \(a\lt b\text{.}\) Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be a bounded 1  function. Let \(a = x_0 \lt x_1\lt \dots \lt x_N = b\) be a subdivision of \([a,b]\text{.}\) We define the upper Riemann sum of \(f\) with respect to this subdivision by
\begin{equation*} U(f; x_0, \dots, x_N) := \sum_{n=1}^N (x_n-x_{n-1}) \sup_{ [x_{n-1}, x_n]} f. \end{equation*}
We define the lower Riemann sum of \(f\) with respect to this subdivision by
\begin{equation*} L(f; x_0, \dots, x_N) := \sum_{n=1}^N (x_n-x_{n-1}) \inf_{ [x_{n-1}, x_n]} f. \end{equation*}
Notice that both the upper and lower Riemann sums are finite real numbers, since the fact that \(f\) is bounded implies that
\begin{align} -\infty \amp \lt (b-a)\inf_{[a,b]} f = \sum_{n=1}^N (x_n-x_{n-1}) \inf_{ [a,b]} f\notag\\ \amp \leq \sum_{n=1}^N (x_n-x_{n-1}) \inf_{ [x_{n-1}, x_n]} f = L(f; x_0, \dots, x_N)\notag\\ \amp\leq \sum_{n=1}^N (x_n-x_{n-1}) \sup_{ [x_{n-1}, x_n]} f = U(f; x_0, \dots, x_N)\notag\\ \amp \leq \sum_{n=1}^N (x_n-x_{n-1}) \sup_{ [a,b]} f \notag\\ \amp = (b-a)\sup_{[a,b]} f\lt \infty.\tag{8.6} \end{align}

(c)

Define the upper/lower Riemann integrals of a function.
Solution.
Definition 8.17. Upper/lower Riemann integral.
Let \(a, b\in \R\) with \(a\lt b\text{.}\) Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be a bounded function. We define the upper Riemann integral of \(f\) to be the infimum of the upper Riemann sum over all possible subdivisions:
\begin{equation*} U(f;[a,b]) := \inf\set{U(f;x_0,\dots, x_N): a= x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = b,\ N \in \N}. \end{equation*}
We define the lower Riemann integral of \(f\) to be the supremum of the lower Riemann sum over all possible subdivisions:
\begin{equation*} L(f;[a,b]) := \sup\set{L(f;x_0,\dots, x_N): a= x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = b,\ N \in \N}. \end{equation*}

(d)

Define what it means for a function to be Riemann integrable and define the Riemann integral.
Solution.
Definition 8.18. Riemann integrable and Riemann integral.
Let \(a, b\in \R\) with \(a\lt b\text{.}\) Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be a bounded function. We say that \(f\) is Riemann 2 integrable on \([a,b]\)} if the upper and lower Riemann integrals coincide, so that
\begin{equation*} U(f;[a,b]) = L(f;[a,b]). \end{equation*}
In this case we define the Riemann integral of \(f\) on \([a,b]\) to be this common value, denoted
\begin{equation*} \int_a^b f \qquad \text{or} \qquad \int_a^b f(x) \intd x. \end{equation*}
We also define
\begin{equation*} \int_b^a f := -\int_a^b f. \end{equation*}

Lecture 8.19. Refining a subdivision.

(a)

Define what it means to be a refinement of a subdivision.
Solution.
Definition 8.20. Refinement of a subdivision.
Let \(a, b\in \R\) with \(a\lt b\text{.}\) We say that the subdivision \(a = y_0 \lt \dots \lt y_{M} = b\) is a refinement of the subdivision \(a = x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_{ N} = b\) if
\begin{equation*} \set{x_0, x_1, \dots, x_N} \subset \set{y_0, y_1,\dots, y_{M}}. \end{equation*}
Given subdivisions \(a = x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_{ N} = b\) and \(a = y_0 \lt \dots \lt y_{M} = b\) (not necessarily refinements of each other) their common refinement is the subdivision \(a = z_0 \lt \dots \lt z_T = b\) obtained by ordering the union
\begin{equation*} \set{x_0, \dots , x_N} \cup \set{y_0, \dots, y_M} = \set{z_0, \dots, z_T}. \end{equation*}

(b)

State and prove how Riemann sums are monotone with respect to refinements.
Solution.
Proposition 8.21. Riemann sums are monotone with respect to refinements.
Let \(a, b\in \R\) with \(a\lt b\text{.}\) Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be a bounded function. Let \(a = y_0 \lt \dots \lt y_M = b\) be a refinement of \(a= x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = b\text{.}\) Then
\begin{align*} L(f;x_0,\dots, x_N)\amp \leq L(f; y_0, \dots, y_M)\\ \amp \leq U(f; y_0, \dots, y_M) \leq U(f;x_0,\dots, x_N). \end{align*}
Proof.
Let \(x_1, x_2, x_3 \in [a,b]\) with \(x_1 \lt x_2 \lt x_3\text{.}\) Since taking a supremum over a larger set does not decrease the supremum, we have
\begin{equation*} \sup_{t \in [x_1, x_2]} f(t) \leq \sup_{t \in [x_1, x_3]} f(t) \quad \text{and} \quad \sup_{t \in [x_2, x_3]} f(t) \leq \sup_{t \in [x_1, x_3]} f(t). \end{equation*}
Therefore
\begin{align*} \amp(x_2 - x_1) \sup_{t \in [x_1, x_2]} f(t) + (x_3 - x_2) \sup_{t \in [x_2, x_3]} f(t)\\ \amp\leq (x_2 - x_1) \sup_{t \in [x_1, x_3]} f(t) + (x_3 - x_2) \sup_{t \in [x_1, x_3]} f(t) = (x_3 - x_1) \sup_{t \in [x_1, x_3]} f(t). \end{align*}
It follows that adding a point to a subdivision does not increase the value of the upper Riemann sum. Repeatedly applying this fact a finite number of times, we deduce that adding a finite number of points to a subdivision does not increase the value of the corresponding Riemann sum. This proves the inequality
\begin{equation*} U(f; y_0, \dots, y_M) \leq U(f;x_0,\dots, x_N). \end{equation*}
The argument for the inequality
\begin{equation*} L(f;x_0,\dots, x_N)\leq L(f; y_0, \dots, y_M) \end{equation*}
is similar (try it!). The inequality
\begin{equation*} L(f; y_0, \dots, y_M) \leq U(f; y_0, \dots, y_M) \end{equation*}
follows from the fact that
\begin{equation*} \inf_{ [y_{n-1}, y_n]} f \leq \sup_{ [y_{n-1}, y_n]} f. \end{equation*}

Lecture 8.22. The "epsilon characterisation" of Riemann integrability.

(a)

State and prove the "epsilon characterisation" of Riemann integrability.
Solution.
Proposition 8.23. Epsilon characterisation of Riemann integrability.
Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be a bounded function. Then \(f\) is Riemann integrable if and only if for every \(\eps > 0\) there exists a subdivision \(a= x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = b\) such that
\begin{equation*} U(f;x_0, \dots, x_N) - L(f;x_0, \dots, x_N)\leq \eps. \end{equation*}
Proof.

First suppose that \(f\) is Riemann integrable. Since decreasing the supremum gives a non-upper bound (Proposition 1.7), there exists a subdivision \(a = y_0 \lt \dots \lt y_M = b\) such that

\begin{equation*} L(f;y_0, \dots, y_N) \geq \int_a^b f - \trecip{2}\eps. \end{equation*}

Since increasing the infimum gives a non lower bound (Proposition 1.7), there exists a subdivision \(a = z_0 \lt \dots \lt z_T = b\) such that

\begin{equation*} U(f;z_0, \dots, z_T) \leq \int_a^b f + \trecip{2}\eps. \end{equation*}

Let \(a= x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = b\) denote the common refinement of \((y_0, \dots, y_M)\) and \((z_0, \dots, z_T)\text{.}\) The required inequalities now follow from the monotonicty of Riemann sums with respect to refinements ( Proposition 8.21).

Next we prove the converse direction. Observe that for any subdivision \(a = x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N\) we have

\begin{equation*} U(f;[a,b]) \leq U(f;x_0, \dots, x_N) \quad \text{and} \quad L(f;[a,b]) \geq L(f;x_0, \dots, x_N) . \end{equation*}

Hence by our assumption, for any \(\eps \gt 0\) we have

\begin{equation*} U(f;[a,b]) -L(f;[a,b]) \leq \eps. \end{equation*}

Since this holds for all \(\eps \gt 0\) we deduce that

\begin{equation*} U(f;[a,b]) \leq L(f;[a,b]). \end{equation*}

The reverse inequality follows from (8.6). Hence \(U(f;[a,b]) = L(f;[a,b])\text{,}\) as required.

See Definition 1.2.
On occasion, we may say "\(f\) is integrable", by which we mean that \(f\) is Riemann integrable.
