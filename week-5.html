    Subsection 5.1 Self-test questions for lecture 9

    - 5.1. Power series.

    (a)
    Define the power series associated to a real sequence.
    Solution.
    Definition 5.2. Power series and radius of convergence.
    Given \(x \in \R\text{,}\) the power series associated to the sequence \((a_n)_{n=0}^\infty\) is the series
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n, \end{equation*}
    provided that this limit exists.
    Example 5.3. The geometric power series.
    The power series associated to the constant sequence \(a_n :=1\) is
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n. \end{equation*}
    This converges absolutely (and is equal to \((1-x)^{-1}\)) when \(|x|\lt 1\text{.}\) It does not converge when \(|x| \geq 1\text{.}\)
    (b)
    State and prove how there exists a radius of convergence for a power series.
    Solution.
    Theorem 5.4. Existence of radius of convergence.
    Let \((a_n)_{n=0}^\infty\) be a sequence of real numbers. There exists \(R \in [0, \infty]\text{,}\) the radius of convergence, for which both of the following hold.
    1. If \(|x| \lt R\) then the power series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n\) is absolutely convergent.
    2. If \(|x| \gt R\) then the power series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n\) does not converge.
    Proof.
    Notice that the set
    \begin{equation*} \set{|x| : \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n \text{ converges}} \end{equation*}
    is non-empty, as it contains \(x=0\text{.}\) If this set is not bounded above, set \(R:=\infty\text{.}\) If this set is bounded above, we can use the completeness principle to take \(R\) to be its supremum.
    If \(\sum_n a_n x^n\) converges then (by definition) \(|x|\leq R\text{.}\) Taking the contrapositive of this implication, we see that if \(|x| \gt R\) then \(\sum_n a_n x^n\) does not converge.
    Suppose that \(|x| \lt R\text{.}\) Since decreasing a supremum gives a non upper bound (Lemma 1.9), there exists \(|y| \gt |x|\) such that \(\sum_n a_n y^n\) converges. Hence, by the \(n\)th term test (Proposition 4.23), we have \(|a_n| |y|^n \to 0\) as \(n\to \infty\text{.}\) In particular, as convergence implies boundedness (Proposition 1.30), there exists \(M\) such that
    \begin{equation*} |a_n| |y|^n \leq M \qquad (\forall n \in \N)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It follows that for all \(n\in \N\) we have
    \begin{equation*} |a_n x^n| = |a_n| |y|^n \brac{|x|/|y|}^n \leq M\brac{|x|/|y|}^n. \end{equation*}
    Since \(|x|/|y| \lt 1\text{,}\) the geometric series \(\sum_n (|x|/|y|)^n\) is convergent (Example 4.3). Hence by the comparison test (Proposition 4.15) the series \(\sum_n a_n x^n\) is absolutely convergent.

    - 5.5. The exponential function.

    Define the exponential function in terms of a power series and justify why this power series converges.
    Solution.
    Definition 5.6. Exponential function.
    The exponential function \(\exp : \R \to \R\) is given by the power series
    \begin{equation*} \exp(x) := \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!}, \end{equation*}
    when this exists. We define the real number \(e \in \R\) via the series
    \begin{equation*} e := \exp(1) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1}{n!}. \end{equation*}
    Proposition 5.7. Radius of convergence for the exponential function.
    The power series
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!} \end{equation*}
    has infinite radius of convergence.
    In particular, the exponential function \(\exp(x)\) is well-defined at all \(x \in \R\text{.}\)
    Proof.
    Let \(N\geq 2|x|\text{.}\) Then for any \(n\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{|x|^{N+n}}{(N+n)!} \leq \frac{|x|^{N}}{N!} \times \frac{|x|^n}{N^n} \leq \frac{|x|^{N}}{N!}\times \brac{\frac{1}{2}}^n. \end{equation*}
    Absolute convergence then follows from the comparison test, as in the following:
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{|x|^n}{n!} = \sum_{n=0}^N \frac{|x|^n}{n!} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{|x|^{N+n}}{(N+n)!} \leq \sum_{n=0}^N \frac{|x|^n}{n!} +\frac{|x|^N}{N!} \sum_{n=1}^\infty 2^{-n} \lt \infty. \end{equation*}

    - 5.8. Multiplying series.

    (a)
    State the "Cauchy product formula" for multiplying two series (the proof is non-examinable).
    Solution.
    Theorem 5.9. Multiplying series.
    Suppose that the series \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n\) and \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n\) are both absolutely convergent. Then we have the Cauchy product formula
    \begin{equation} \brac{\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n}\brac{\sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \brac{\sum_{r+s = n} a_rb_s}.\tag{5.1} \end{equation}
    Furthermore the right-hand side is absolutely convergent.
    Here we are using the notation
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{r+s = n} a_rb_s := \sum_{r=0}^n a_rb_{n-r} = a_0b_n + a_1b_{n-1} + \dots + a_nb_0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Proof (non-examinable).
    By the multiplication rule for limits (Proposition 2.17) we have that
    \begin{equation*} \brac{\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n }\brac{\sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n} = \lim_{N\to \infty } \brac{\brac{\sum_{n=0}^N a_n}\brac{\sum_{n=0}^N b_n}}. \end{equation*}
    One can check (using the addition rule for limits Proposition 2.14), that it therefore suffices to show that
    \begin{equation} \brac{\sum_{n=0}^N a_n}\brac{\sum_{n=0}^N b_n} - \sum_{n=0}^N \brac{\sum_{r+s = n} a_rb_s} \to 0 \quad \text{ as }\quad N \to \infty \text{.}\tag{5.2} \end{equation}
    Our proof of (5.2) starts by multiplying out the partial sums and grouping the terms \(a_rb_s\) according to the value of \(r+s\text{,}\) as follows
    \begin{align*} \brac{\sum_{n=0}^N a_n}\brac{\sum_{n=0}^N b_n} \amp = \sum_{0\leq r,s\leq N} a_rb_s = \sum_{n=0}^{2N} \ \sum_{\substack{0 \leq r, s \leq N\\ r+s = n}} a_rb_s \\ \amp = \sum_{n=0}^{N}\ \sum_{r+s = n} a_rb_s + \sum_{n=N+1}^{2N} \ \sum_{\substack{0 \leq r, s \leq N\\ r+s = n}} a_rb_s\text{.} \end{align*}
    To prove (5.2) it therefore suffices to prove that
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=N+1}^{2N} \ \sum_{\substack{0 \leq r, s \leq N\\ r+s = n}} a_rb_s \to 0 \quad\text{ as } \quad N \to \infty. \end{equation*}
    We observe that if \(0 \leq r, s \leq N\) and \(r+s = n\) for some \(n \in (N, 2N]\) then either \(r \geq N/2\) or \(s \geq N/2\text{.}\) Hence, on writing
    \begin{equation*} A:=\sum_{n=0}^\infty |a_n| \quad \text{and}\quad B:=\sum_{n=0}^\infty |b_n|, \end{equation*}
    the triangle inequality and the inequality rule (Proposition 2.5) together give that
    \begin{align*} \abs{\sum_{n=N+1}^{2N} \ \sum_{\substack{0 \leq r, s \leq N\\ r+s = n}} a_rb_s} \amp \leq \sum_{r = \ceil{\frac{N}{2}}}^N |a_r|\sum_{s=0}^N |b_s|+ \sum_{r=0}^N |a_r|\sum_{s = \ceil{\frac{N}{2}}}^N |b_s|\\ \amp \leq B\sum_{r = \ceil{\frac{N}{2}}}^\infty |a_r|+ A\sum_{s = \ceil{\frac{N}{2}}}^\infty |b_s|\text{.} \end{align*}
    By absolute convergence we have
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{r =N}^\infty |a_r| = \sum_{r=0}^\infty|a_r| - \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} |a_r| \to 0 \text{ as } N \to \infty. \end{equation*}
    Since convergence is preserved on passing to a subsequence (Proposition 3.20) we deduce that
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{r = \ceil{\frac{N}{2}}}^\infty |a_r| \to 0 \text{ as } N \to \infty. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, by the multiplication rule for limits (Proposition 2.17) and using the fact that \(B = \sum_s |b_s|\) is a fixed finite real number, we have
    \begin{equation*} B\sum_{r = \ceil{\frac{N}{2}}}^\infty |a_r| \to 0 \text{ as } N \to \infty. \end{equation*}
    The proof is complete on employing a similar argument to show that
    \begin{equation*} A\sum_{s = \ceil{\frac{N}{2}}}^\infty |b_s| \to 0 \text{ as } N \to \infty. \end{equation*}
    (b)
    State and prove the multiplication rule for the exponential function.
    Solution.
    Proposition 5.10.
    The exponential function satisfies the identity
    \begin{equation*} \exp(x+y) = \exp(x)\exp(y), \qquad (\forall x,y \in \R). \end{equation*}
    Proof.
    By the Cauchy product formula for multiplying absolutely convergent series (Theorem 5.9) we have
    \begin{equation*} \exp(x)\exp(y) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \sum_{r+s= n} \frac{x^ry^s}{r!s!} \end{equation*}
    By the binomial theorem
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{r+s = n} \frac{x^ry^s}{r!s!} = \sum_{r=0}^n \frac{x^r y^{n-r}}{r!(n-r)!}= \recip{n!}\sum_{r=0}^n \binom{n}{r}x^r y^{n-r} = \recip{n!} (x+y)^n. \end{equation*}

    Subsection 5.2 Self-test questions for lecture 10

    - 5.11. Limits of functions.

    (a)
    Given a real-valued function \(f\) on a real domain \(D\text{,}\) define the meaning of the statement:
    \begin{equation*} f(x) \to y \quad \text{as} \quad x \to x_0\quad \text{through} \quad D. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Definition 5.12. Sequential definition of function limit.
    Given a real-valued function \(f: D \to \R\) on a real domain \(D\text{,}\) we write
    \begin{equation*} f(x) \to y \quad \text{as} \quad x \to x_0 \quad \text{through} \quad D \end{equation*}
    if, for any sequence \((a_n)_n\) in 1  \(D\) we have the implication
    \begin{equation} a_n \to x_0 \quad \implies \quad f(a_n) \to y.\tag{5.3} \end{equation}
    We also denote this as
    \begin{equation*} y = \lim_{x\to x_0; x \in D} f(x) \end{equation*}
    Remark 5.13.
    If the domain \(D\) is clear from the context, then we may drop the "through \(D\)" part, and simply write
    \begin{equation*} f(x) \to y \quad \text{as} \quad x \to x_0, \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} y = \lim_{x\to x_0} f(x) \end{equation*}
    Remark 5.14.
    Notice that our definition of the limit is not the epsilon-delta definition which you may be more familiar with. If you are asked to define the limit, you should use the sequential definition above. Our reason for preferring the sequential definition is that it builds on the theory of sequences which begins this course, and also allows for the limit point \(x_0\) to be \(\pm\infty\text{.}\) The epsilon-delta definition does not include the latter possibility.
    We will soon deduce that the epsilon-delta characterisation is equivalent to the sequential definition when \(x_0,y\in \R\) are both finite, but the epsilon-delta characterisation is not as general.
    Example 5.15.
    Consider \(f:(0, \infty) \to \R\) given by \(f(x):=1/x\text{.}\) We claim that
    \begin{equation*} f(x) \to \infty \quad \text{as}\quad x\to 0 \quad \text{through}\quad (0, \infty). \end{equation*}
    To establish this claim, let \((a_n)_n\) be a sequence in \((0, \infty)\) with \(a_n \to 0\) as \(n\to \infty\text{.}\) We must verify that \(1/a_n\to \infty\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\) The latter follows from the reciprocal rule for sequences of positive numbers tending to infinity (Proposition 3.11).
    Notice, however, that it is not the case that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x} \to \infty \quad \text{as}\quad x\to 0 \quad \text{through}\quad \R\setminus\set{0}. \end{equation*}
    This is because, for instance, the sequences \((1/n)_n\) and \((-1/n)_n\) are both contained in \(\R\setminus\set{0}\) and both tend to \(0\text{,}\) yet
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{1/n} \to +\infty \qquad \text{and}\qquad \frac{1}{-1/n} \to -\infty\text{.} \end{equation*}

    - 5.16. The epsilon-delta characterisation of a finite limit at a finite point.

    (a)
    State and prove the "epsilon-delta" characterisation of the limit, when the limit point and the limiting value are both finite.
    Solution.
    Proposition 5.17. Epsilon-delta characterisation of the limit.
    Let \(f : D \to \R\) be a real valued function on a real domain \(D \subset \R\) and let \(x_0,y\in \R\) be finite real numbers. Then we have
    \begin{equation*} f(x)\to y \quad \text{as}\quad x \to x_0 \quad\text{through}\quad D \end{equation*}
    if and only if
    \begin{align} (\forall \eps \gt 0)(\exists \delta=\delta(x_0,\eps) \gt 0)\amp(\forall x \in D)\notag\\ \amp \bigsqbrac{\ |x-x_0| \leq \delta \implies |f(x)-y| \leq \eps\ }.\tag{5.4} \end{align}
    Proof.
    We first prove the "\(\impliedby\)" direction. Let \((a_n)_n\) be a sequence in \(D\) such that \(a_n \to x_0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\) We are required to prove that \(f(a_n) \to y\) as \(n \to \infty\text{,}\) assuming (5.4). Fix \(\eps \gt0\text{.}\) Then by (5.4) there exists \(\delta = \delta(\eps) \gt 0\) such that if \(x \in D\) and \(|x-x_0| \leq \delta\) then \(|f(x)-y|\leq \eps\text{.}\) Since \(a_n \to x_0\) there exists \(N = N(\delta)\) such that for all \(n\geq N\) we have \(|a_n - x_0| \leq \delta\text{.}\) Hence if \(n \geq N(\delta)\) then \(|f(a_n)-y| \leq \eps\text{.}\) This establishes that \(f(a_n) \to y\text{.}\)
    Next we prove the "\(\implies\)" direction. To do so we prove the contrapositive. Hence, suppose that the negation of (5.4) holds:
    \begin{align*} (\exists \eps \gt 0)(\forall \delta \gt 0)\amp(\exists x= x(\delta) \in D)\\ \amp \bigsqbrac{\ |x-x_0| \leq \delta\quad \text{and}\quad |f(x)-y| \gt \eps\ }. \end{align*}
    Taking \(\delta = 1/n\) in the above negation, set \(a_1:= x(1)\text{,}\) \(a_2:=x(1/2)\text{,}\) \(a_3 := x(1/3)\text{,}\) etc. Then \(a_n \in D\text{,}\) \(|a_n - x_0| \leq 1/n\) and \(|f(a_n)-y| \gt \eps\text{.}\) Hence \((a_n)_n\) is a sequence in \(D\) with \(a_n \to x_0\) and \(f(a_n) \not\to y\) (as required).

    - 5.18. Inequalities spread around a limit.

    State and prove how a function whose limit at a point satisfies an inequality also satisfies the inequality on a neighbourhood of that point.
    Solution.
    Proposition 5.19. Inequalities spread around a limit.
    Let \(f : D \to \R\) be a real-valued function on a real domain \(D \subset \R\) and let \(x_0 \in [-\infty, \infty]\text{.}\) Suppose that there exists \(y\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f(x)\to y\quad\text{as}\quad x\to x_0 \quad\text{through}\quad D. \end{equation*}
    If \(y\gt z\) then there exists a neighbourhood 2  \(N\) of \(x_0\) such that for all \(x \in D\cap N\) we have \(f(x) \gt z \text{.}\) (An analogous result hold if \(y \lt z\text{,}\) with inequalities reversed.)
    Proof.
    We prove the case in which \(x_0, y, z \in \R\) and leave the infinite cases to the reader.
    Take \(\eps := \frac{y-z}{2}\) in (5.4). Then by the triangle inequality, for \(x \in D\) satisfying \(|x-x_0| \leq \delta = \delta(\eps)\) we have
    \begin{align*} f(x) = y + f(x) - y \geq y- |f(x)-y| \amp \geq y - \eps\\ \amp = z + \eps \gt z. \end{align*}

    - 5.20. Continuity.

    Define what it means for a real-valued function on a real domain to be continuous at a point and what it means for the function to be continuous.
    Solution.
    Definition 5.21. Continuous.
    Let \(f : D \to \R\) be a real valued function on a real domain \(D \subset \R\text{.}\) Let \(x_0\in D\text{.}\) We say that \(f : D \to \R\) is continuous at \(x_0\) if \(f(x) \to f(x_0)\) as \(x \to x_0\) through \(D\text{.}\) We say that \(f : D \to \R\) is continuous on the set \(D_0\subset D\) if it is continuous at all \(x_0 \in D_0\) (when \(D_0 = D\) we simply say "\(f\) is continuous").
    Example 5.22. Constant functions are continuous.
    Let \(f : D \to \R\) with \(D \subset \R\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f\) is constant. In other words, there exists \(c \in \R\) such that for all \(x \in D\) we have \(f(x) = c\text{.}\) Then for any sequence \((a_n)_n\) in \(D\) we have \(f(a_n) \to c\text{,}\) by Example 1.25. Hence \(f\) is continuous on \(D\text{.}\)
    Example 5.23. The identity function is continuous.
    Let \(D \subset \R\text{.}\) Define \(f : D \to \R\) by \(f(x) := x\) for all \(x \in D\text{.}\) Then for any sequence \((a_n)_n\) in \(D\) with \(a_n \to x_0\) we have \(f(a_n) = a_n \to x_0 = f(x_0)\text{.}\) Hence \(f\) is continuous on \(D\text{.}\)
    Example 5.24. A function discontinuous at a point.
    Consider the function \(f : \R \to \R\) given by
    \begin{equation*} f(x) := \begin{cases} 0 \amp \text{ if } x \leq 0;\\ 1\amp \text{ if } x \gt0. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    We claim that this function is not continuous at \(x_0 = 0\text{.}\) In order to have \(f(x) \to f(0)\) as \(x \to 0\) we need \(f(a_n) \to 0\) for all sequences \((a_n)_n\) for which \(a_n \to 0\text{.}\) Yet \(1/n \to 0\) and \(f(1/n) \to 1\text{.}\)
    Example 5.25. The absolute value function is continuous.
    By the triangle inequality, for any \(x, x_0 \in \R\) we have
    \begin{equation*} |x-x_0| \leq \eps \implies ||x|-|x_0|| \leq \eps. \end{equation*}
    Hence (5.4) holds for the function \(x \mapsto |x|\) on taking \(\delta(x_0, \eps) := \eps\text{.}\)
    Example 5.26. A function discontinuous everywhere.
    Consider the function \(f : \R \to \R\) given by
    \begin{equation*} f(x) := \begin{cases} 1 \amp \text{ if } x \in \Q;\\ 0\amp \text{ if } x \in \R \setminus\Q. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    We claim that this function is not continuous at any \(x_0 \in \R\text{.}\) Notice, however, that the restrictions 3  \(f|_{\Q}\) and \(f|_{\R\setminus\Q}\) are both continuous, since they are constant functions. The key observation in establishing our claim is to note that every real number \(x_0\) has a sequence of rationals \(q_n \in \Q\) such that \(q_n \to x_0\text{.}\) For instance, one may take
    \begin{equation*} q_n := \frac{\floor{nx_0}}{n} \in \left(x_0 -\frac{1}{n}, x_0\right]\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and employ the two-sided sandwich rule (Proposition 2.10). Let us use this to show that our function is not continuous at \(x_0\text{.}\) First suppose that \(x_0\in \R\setminus\Q\text{.}\) It follows that \(f(q_n) = 1 \not\to 0 = f(x_0)\text{.}\) Next suppose that \(x_0 \in \Q\text{.}\) This time, let \(q_n \in \Q\) be a sequence of rationals such that \(q_n \to x_0+ \sqrt{2}\text{.}\) Notice that for any \(n \in \N\) we have \(q_n-\sqrt{2} \notin \Q\text{,}\) otherwise \(\sqrt{2} \in \Q\) (a contradiction). Also \(q_n -\sqrt{2} \to x_0\text{.}\) Yet \(f(q_n-\sqrt{2}) = 0 \not\to 1 = f(x_0)\text{.}\)

    Subsection 5.3 Workshop questions

    - 5.27. True/false workshop quiz.

    In your workshop, you will vote on whether you think each of the following statements is true (thumbs up) or false (thumbs down). For each question, your workshop tutor will ask one of you to briefly justify your vote. This justification can involve giving a counterexample (if false) or outlining which definition/theorem the statement follows from (if true).
    (a)
    We say that a series \(\sum_n a_n\) converges if \(a_n \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\)
    (b)
    If \(a_n \lt 0\) for all \(n \in \N\) then the series \(\sum_n a_n\) exists.
    (c)
    The series \(\sum_n a_n\) converges absolutely if and only if \(\sum_n|a_n| \lt \infty\text{.}\)
    (d)
    If \(\sum_n a_n \in (0, \infty)\) and \(\sum_n b_n \in (-\infty, 0)\) then \(\sum_n (a_n+b_n) = 0\text{.}\)
    (e)
    If \(a_n \leq 2^{-n}\) for all \(n \in \N\) then \(\sum_n a_n \) converges.
    (f)
    If \(|a_n| > 2^{-n}\) for all \(n \in \N\) then \(\sum_n a_n \) does not converge absolutely.
    (g)
    Suppose that \((a_n)_n\) is a sequence of non-negative reals. If \(\sum_n (-1)^{n-1}a_n\) converges then the sequence \((a_n)_n\) is decreasing and converges to zero.
    (h)

    - 5.30. Exam-style self-test question for workshop.

    The purpose of this question is to use the sequential definition of the limit to give an alternative proof of the fact that a function with a positive limit at a point must be positive on a neighbourhood of that point. Let \(f: D \to \R\) be a real-valued function on a real domain \(D \subset \R\) and let \(x_0\in \R\text{.}\)
    (a)
    Let \(y \in \R\text{.}\) Give the (sequential) definition of what it means to have \(f(x) \to y\) as \(x\to x_0\) through \(D\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    This is Task 5.11.a.
    (b)
    Suppose that \(f:D\to \R\) has domain \(D = \R\text{.}\) What is the logical negation of the statement:
    \begin{equation} (\exists \delta \gt 0)(\forall x \in [-\delta,\delta])[f(x) \gt 0]\tag{5.5} \end{equation}
    Solution.
    \begin{equation} (\forall \delta \gt 0)(\exists x \in [-\delta,\delta])[f(x) \leq 0]\tag{5.6} \end{equation}
    (c)
    Using the notation and assumptions from the previous part, suppose that (5.5) does not hold. Justify why for any \(n \in \N\) there exists \(|x_n|\leq 1/n\) such that \(f(x_n)\leq 0\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Set \(\delta:=1/n\) in (5.6).
    (d)
    Using the notation and assumptions from the previous part, suppose that (5.5) does not hold and that (in addition) \(f\) is continuous at \(0\text{.}\) Deduce that it is not the case that \(f(0) \gt 0\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Using the previous part, we obtain a sequence \((x_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) such that \(x_n \to 0\) (by the sandwich rule) and \(f(x_n)\leq 0\) for all \(n\in \N\text{.}\) By continuity and the inequality rule for sequential limits, we thereby deduce that
    \begin{equation*} f(0) = \lim_{n\to \infty} f(x_n) \leq 0. \end{equation*}
