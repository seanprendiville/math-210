    Section 4 Week 4

    - 4.1. Infinite series and convergence.

    Given a sequence of real numbers, define the corresponding \(N\)th partial sum and infinite series. When do we say the infinite series exists? What is a term of an infinite series? What does it mean to say a series converges?
    Solution.

    Definition 4.2. Infinite series.

    Given a sequence of real numbers \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) and \(N\in \N\text{,}\) the \(N\)th partial sum is given by
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^N a_n := a_1 + a_2 + \dots + a_N. \end{equation*}
    If the sequence of partial sums tends to a limit as \(N \to \infty\text{,}\) then the corresponding infinite series exists and is equal to the limit
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{N\to\infty} \sum_{n=1}^Na_n, \quad \text{written}\quad \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n. \end{equation*}
    The terms of the infinite series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) are the summands \(a_n\text{.}\)
    If the limit \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n=\lim_{N\to \infty} \sum_{n=1}^N a_n\) exists and equals a finite real number, then we say that the series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \) is convergent.

    Example 4.3. Geometric series.

    If \(|x|\lt 1\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n = \frac{1}{1-x}. \end{equation*}
    Proof.
    We have
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^N x^n = 1 + \brac{x\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x^n} = 1+ \brac{x\sum_{n=0}^N x^n}- x^{N+1} . \end{equation*}
    Re-arranging (provided that \(x \neq 1\)) gives
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^N x^n = \frac{1-x^{N+1}}{1-x}. \end{equation*}
    If \(|x| \lt 1\) then \(x^N \to 0\) as \(N\to \infty\) by Proposition 3.14 and Proposition 3.11. By the algebra of limits (Proposition 2.14, Proposition 2.17 and Proposition 2.21) we have
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{n=0}^N x^n = \lim_{N \to \infty} \frac{1-x^{N+1}}{1-x} = \frac{1 - x\displaystyle\lim_{N\to \infty} x^N}{1-x} = \frac{1}{1-x}. \end{equation*}

    Example 4.4. A series which does not converge but which exists.

    Set \(a_n := 1/2\) for \(n=1, 2, \dots\text{.}\) Then the partial sums satisfy
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^N a_n = \frac{N}{2} \to \infty \quad \text{as } N \to \infty. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty 1/2\) exists, but is not convergent (it equals \(\infty\)).

    Example 4.5. A series which does not exist.

    Set \(a_n := (-1)^n\) for \(n=1, 2, \dots\text{.}\) Then the partial sums satisfy \(\sum_{n=1}^N a_n = -1\) if \(N\) is odd and \(\sum_{n=1}^N a_n = 0\) if \(N\) is even. It follows that the sequence of partial sums has a subsequence with limit \(-1\) and a subsequence with limit \(0\text{.}\) Therefore the sequence of partial sums cannot tend to a limit, since if a sequence has a limit all its subsequences tend to the same limit (Proposition 3.20). Thus \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^n\) does not exist.

    - 4.6. Series of non-negative terms are well-defined.

    Justify why an infinite series always exists when the terms are non-negative.
    Solution.

    Proposition 4.7. Series of non-negative terms always exist.

    Let \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be a non-negative sequence, so that \(a_n \geq 0\) for all \(n\in \N\text{.}\) Then the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) exists, although it may equal \(\infty\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Since \(a_n \geq 0\) for all \(n\in \N\text{,}\) the sequence of partial sums
    \begin{equation*} a_1 \quad \leq\quad a_1 + a_2 \quad \leq\quad a_1 + a_2 + a_3 \quad \leq\quad \dots \end{equation*}
    is an increasing sequence of real numbers. Hence by the monotone convergence principle (Proposition 2.3), the limit
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n = \lim_{N\to \infty} \sum_{n=1}^Na_n \end{equation*}
    always exists, but is possibly \(\infty\text{.}\)

    Remark 4.8.

    We write
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \lt \infty \end{equation*}
    to indicate that the series converges to a real number (when the underlying sequence \((a_n)\) is non-negative).

    - 4.9. Absolute convergence.

    (a)

    Define what it means for an infinite series to be absolutely convergent.
    Solution.
    Definition 4.10. Absolute convergence.
    We say that the series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) is absolutely convergent if
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty |a_n| \lt \infty. \end{equation*}

    (b)

    State and prove how absolute convergence implies convergence.
    Solution.
    Proposition 4.11. Absolute convergence implies convergence.
    Let \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be a sequence of real numbers. If \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty |a_n| \lt \infty\) then the series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) converges to a finite real number.
    In addition, we have the infinite triangle inequality
    \begin{equation} \abs{\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n }\leq \sum_{n=1}^\infty |a_n|.\tag{4.1} \end{equation}
    Proof.
    Recall that the limit \(\sum_n |a_n|\) always exists (but is possibly infinite), by the monotone convergence principle (Proposition 2.3), as in Proposition 4.7. To prove that the series \(\sum_n a_n\) converges, it suffices to prove that the sequence of partial sums \(\sum_{n=1}^N a_n\) is a Cauchy sequence (Definition 3.27), since Cauchy sequences converge (Proposition 3.28). To this end, let \(\eps \gt 0\text{.}\) Since we are assuming that \(\sum_{n=1}^N |a_n|\) tends to a real limit as \(N \to \infty\text{,}\) we know that this is a Cauchy sequence (Proposition 3.28). Hence there exists \(N_0 \in \N\) such that for all \(M\gt N \geq N_0\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \eps \geq \abs{\sum_{n=1}^M |a_n| - \sum_{n=1}^N |a_n|} = \sum_{n=N+1}^M |a_n|. \end{equation*}
    By the triangle inequality, for all \(M \gt N \geq N_0\) we have that
    \begin{equation*} \abs{ \sum_{n=1}^M a_n - \sum_{n=1}^N a_n} = \abs{\sum_{n=N+1}^M a_n} \leq \sum_{n=N+1}^M |a_n| \leq \eps. \end{equation*}
    This establishes the Cauchy property, as claimed.
    To prove the infinite triangle inequality (4.1) we first observe the finite triangle inequality
    \begin{equation*} -\sum_{n=1}^N |a_n| \leq \sum_{n=1}^N a_n \leq \sum_{n=1}^N |a_n| \end{equation*}
    The infinite triangle inequality follows on letting \(N \to \infty\) and applying the inequality rule for limits (Proposition 2.5) to either side of the above.

    - 4.12. Adding and dilating series.

    State and prove how infinite series respect both addition and scalar multiplication.
    Solution.

    Proposition 4.13. Linearity of series.

    Suppose that the series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) and \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n\) both converge. Let \(\lambda, \mu \in \R\text{.}\) Then the series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty (\lambda a_n + \mu b_n)\) converges and satisfies the identity
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty (\lambda a_n + \mu b_n) = \lambda \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n + \mu \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    This is an application of the addition and multiplication rules for sequences (Proposition 2.14 and Proposition 2.17), as follows:
    \begin{align*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty (\lambda a_n + \mu b_n) \amp = \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{n=1}^N (\lambda a_n + \mu b_n)\qquad \text{[definition of series]}\\ \amp = \lim_{N \to \infty}\brac{ \lambda {\sum_{n=1}^N a_n} + \mu{\sum_{n=1}^N b_n}}\qquad \text{[linearity of finite sums]}\\ \amp = \lim_{N \to \infty}\brac{ \lambda\sum_{n=1}^N a_n} + \lim_{N \to \infty}\brac{\mu\sum_{n=1}^N b_n} \qquad \text{[addition rule]}\\ \amp = \lambda \lim_{N \to \infty}\sum_{n=1}^N a_n + \mu \lim_{N \to \infty}\sum_{n=1}^N b_n\qquad \text{[multiplication rule]}. \end{align*}

    - 4.14. The comparison test.

    State and prove the "comparison test" for convergence of infinite series.
    Solution.

    Proposition 4.15. Comparison test.

    Let \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) and \((b_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be sequences of real numbers. Suppose that there exists a constant \(C\) such that for all \(n\) we have \(|a_n| \leq Cb_n\text{.}\) If
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n \lt \infty. \end{equation*}
    Then \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) converges absolutely.

    Proof.

    Notice that we must have \(b_n \geq 0\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Hence for any \(N\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^N |a_n| \leq \sum_{n=1}^N Cb_n \leq C\sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n. \end{equation*}
    Therefore the sequence of partial sums \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^N |a_n|\) is an increasing sequence bounded above by the real number \(\displaystyle C \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n\text{.}\) Hence, by the monotone convergence principle (Proposition 2.3), the sequence of partial sums \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^N |a_n|\) converges to a real number. This proves absolute convergence (and hence convergence by Proposition 4.11).

    Example 4.16. An example of applying the comparison test.

    We claim that
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{100n}{3^n} \lt \infty. \end{equation*}
    To establish this we employ the comparison test (Proposition 4.15) with \(a_n := \frac{100n}{3^n}\text{,}\) \(b_n := \brac{\frac{2}{3}}^n\) and \(C:= \frac{50}{3}\text{,}\) so that it suffices to establish the inequality
    \begin{equation} \frac{100n}{3^n} \leq 50 \times \brac{\frac{2}{3}}^n \qquad (n \in \N).\tag{4.2} \end{equation}
    To this end, we first observe that for \(n\geq 1\) we have
    \begin{equation*} a_{n+1} = \frac{100(n+1)}{3^{n+1}} \leq \frac{100(n+n)}{3\times3^{n}} = \frac{2}{3}\times \frac{100n}{3^{n}} = \frac{2}{3} a_n. \end{equation*}
    It follows that for all \(n\) we have
    \begin{equation*} a_n \leq \frac{2}{3} a_{n-1} \leq \brac{\frac{2}{3}}^2 a_{n-2} \leq \dots \leq \brac{\frac{2}{3}}^{n-1} a_{1} = 50 \brac{\frac{2}{3}}^{n}. \end{equation*}
    The claimed inequality (4.2) follows.

    - 4.17. Alternating series test.

    State the "alternating series test" for convergence of infinite series (the proof is non-examinable).
    Solution.

    Proposition 4.18. Alternating series test.

    Let \((a_n)_n\) denote a decreasing sequence of non-negative reals, so that \(a_n \geq a_{n+1} \geq 0\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Suppose that \(a_n \to 0\text{.}\) Then the series
    \begin{equation*} a_1-a_2+a_3-a_4 + \dots = \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1} a_n \end{equation*}
    converges.

    Proof (NON-EXAMINABLE).

    Before proving the alternating series test, we prove the following key inequality, whose statement and proof are both NON-EXAMINABLE.

    Lemma 4.19.

    For any decreasing sequence \(b_1 \geq b_2 \geq \dots \geq b_n\geq 0\) of non-negative reals, with finitely many terms, we have the inequalities
    \begin{equation} 0 \leq b_1 - b_2 + \dots + (-1)^{n+1} b_n \leq b_1.\tag{4.3} \end{equation}

    Proof of Lemma 4.19.

    We prove this by induction on \(n\text{,}\) the number of terms in the sequence. The basis case, when \(n=1\text{,}\) follows from the inequalities
    \begin{equation*} 0 \leq b_1 \leq b_1. \end{equation*}
    For the inductive step, we may suppose that
    \begin{equation*} 0 \leq b_2 -b_3 + \dots + (-1)^n b_{n} \leq b_2. \end{equation*}
    It follows that
    \begin{equation*} b_1 - b_2 + \dots + (-1)^{n+1} b_n = b_1- \brac{b_2 -b_3+ \dots + (-1)^{n} b_n} \geq b_1-b_2 \geq 0 \end{equation*}
    and that
    \begin{equation*} b_1 - b_2 + \dots + (-1)^{n+1} b_n = b_1- \brac{b_2 -b_3+ \dots + (-1)^{n} b_n} \leq b_1- 0= b_1. \end{equation*}

    Proof of alternating series test (Proposition 4.18).

    Applying the inequality (4.3), for any \(N \gt M\) we have
    \begin{align*} \abs{\sum_{n=1}^N(-1)^{n+1} a_n -\sum_{n=1}^M(-1)^{n+1} a_n } \amp= \abs{ \sum_{n=M+1}^N (-1)^{n+1} a_n}\\ \amp = a_{M+1} - a_{M+2} + \dots + (-1)^{N-M+1} a_N\\ \amp \leq a_{M+1}. \end{align*}
    Since \(a_{n} \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\) there exists \(N_0 = N_0(\eps)\) such that for all \(n \geq N_0(\eps)\) we have \(a_{n} \leq \eps\text{.}\) In particular, if \(N \gt M \geq N_0(\eps)\) then
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\sum_{n=1}^N(-1)^{n+1} a_n -\sum_{n=1}^M(-1)^{n+1} a_n } \leq a_{M+1} \leq \eps. \end{equation*}
    It follows that the sequence of partial sums
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^N (-1)^{n+1} a_n \end{equation*}
    is a Cauchy sequence (Definition 3.27), hence convergent (see Proposition 3.28).

    Example 4.20. An example of applying the alternating series test.

    We claim that the series
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n} \end{equation*}
    converges, even though it is not absolutely convergent. To establish this we employ the alternating series test (Proposition 4.18) with \(a_n := \frac{1}{n}\text{,}\) so that it suffices to establish that the sequence \((a_n)_n\) is non-negative, decreasing and converges to 0 (we leave proving this to the reader).

    Example 4.21. An example of an alternating series which converges, but whose terms are not decreasing in absolute value.

    Consider the sequence
    \begin{equation*} a_n := \begin{cases} 2^{-n} \amp \text{if } n \text{ odd};\\ 3^{-n} \amp \text{if } n \text{ even}. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Then the sequence \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) is not decreasing, yet the corresponding alternating series converges, since by addition of convergent series (Proposition 4.13) we have
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1} a_n = \sum_{m=1}^\infty 2^{-(2m-1)} - \sum_{m=1}^\infty 3^{2m} = \frac{2}{3}-\frac{1}{8}. \end{equation*}

    - 4.22. \(n\)th term test.

    State and prove the "\(n\)th term test" for non-convergence of infinite series.
    Solution.

    Proposition 4.23. \(n\)th term test.

    Suppose that the sequence \((a_n)_n\) does not tend to zero, written \(a_n \not\to 0\) (or equivalently \(|a_n| \not\to 0\)). Then the series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) does not converge.

    Proof.

    We prove the contrapositive 1 . To this end, suppose that \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) converges to a real number. Then by Proposition 3.28, the sequence of partial sums
    \begin{equation*} \brac{\sum_{n=1}^{N} a_n}_{N=1}^\infty \end{equation*}
    is a Cauchy sequence. Hence for any \(\eps \gt 0\) there exists \(N_0 = N_0(\eps)\) such that for all \(N, M\geq N_0(\eps)\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\sum_{n=1}^M a_n - \sum_{n=1}^N a_n} \leq \eps. \end{equation*}
    In particular, if \(N-1 \geq N_0(\eps)\) then we have
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\sum_{n=1}^N a_n - \sum_{n=1}^{N-1} a_n}\leq \eps. \end{equation*}
    It follows that \(a_N \to 0\) according to Definition 1.23.
