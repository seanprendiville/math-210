Section 4 Week 4

Lecture 4.1. Infinite series.

(a)

Given a sequence of real numbers, define the corresponding \(N\)th partial sum and infinite series. When do we say the infinite series exists? What is a term of an infinite series?
Solution.
Definition 4.2. Infinite series.
Given a sequence of real numbers \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) and \(N\in \N\text{,}\) the \(N\)th partial sum is given by
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^N a_n := a_1 + a_2 + \dots + a_N. \end{equation*}
If the sequence of partial sums tends to a limit as \(N \to \infty\text{,}\) then the corresponding infinite series exists and is equal to the limit
\begin{equation*} \lim_{N\to\infty} \sum_{n=1}^Na_n, \quad \text{written}\quad \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n. \end{equation*}
The terms of the infinite series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) are the summands \(a_n\text{.}\)

(b)

Justify why an infinite series always exists when the terms are non-negative.
Solution.
Remark 4.3.
Suppose that the sequence \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) is non-negative, so that \(a_n \geq 0\) for all \(n\in \N\text{.}\) Then the sequence of partial sums
\begin{equation*} a_1 \quad \leq\quad a_1 + a_2 \quad \leq\quad a_1 + a_2 + a_3 \quad \leq\quad \dots \end{equation*}
is an increasing sequence of real numbers. Hence by the monotone convergence principle (Proposition 3.3), the limit
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n = \lim_{N\to \infty} \sum_{n=1}^Na_n \end{equation*}
always exists, but is possibly \(\infty\text{.}\) We write
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \lt \infty \end{equation*}
to indicate that the series converges to a real number (when the underlying sequence \((a_n)\) is non-negative).

(c)

Define what it means for an infinite series to be convergent. Give both an example and non-example, with proof.
Solution.
Let \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be a sequence of real numbers. If the limit \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n=\lim_{N\to \infty} \sum_{n=1}^N a_n\) exists and equals a finite real number, then we say that the series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \) is convergent.

(d)

Define what it means for an infinite series to be absolutely convergent. Given both an example and a non-example, with proof.
Solution.
We say that the series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) is absolutely convergent if
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty |a_n| \lt \infty. \end{equation*}
We say that the series is conditionally covergent if it is convergent but not absolutely convergent.

(e)

State and prove the value of the geometric series.
Solution.
Example 4.4. Geometric series.
If \(|x|\lt 1\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n = \frac{1}{1-x}. \end{equation*}
Proof.
We have
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^N x^n = 1 + \brac{x\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x^n} = 1+ \brac{x\sum_{n=0}^N x^n}- x^{N+1} . \end{equation*}
Re-arranging (provided that \(x \neq 1\)) gives
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^N x^n = \frac{1-x^{N+1}}{1-x}. \end{equation*}
If \(|x| \lt 1\) then \(x^N \to 0\) as \(N\to \infty\) by Proposition 3.6. By the algebra of limits (Proposition 2.8, Proposition 2.10 and Proposition 2.12) we have
\begin{equation*} \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{n=0}^N x^n = \lim_{N \to \infty} \frac{1-x^{N+1}}{1-x} = \frac{1 - x\displaystyle\lim_{N\to \infty} x^N}{1-x} = \frac{1}{1-x}. \end{equation*}

(f)

State and prove how infinite series respect both addition and scalar multiplication.
Solution.
Proposition 4.5. Linearity of series.
Suppose that the series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) and \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n\) both converge. Let \(\lambda, \mu \in \R\text{.}\) Then the series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty (\lambda a_n + \mu b_n)\) converges and satisfies the identity
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty (\lambda a_n + \mu b_n) = \lambda \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n + \mu \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n. \end{equation*}
Proof.
This is an application of the addition and multiplication rules for sequences (Proposition 2.8 and Proposition 2.10), as follows:
\begin{align*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty (\lambda a_n + \mu b_n) \amp = \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{n=1}^N (\lambda a_n + \mu b_n)\qquad \text{[definition of series]}\\ \amp = \lim_{N \to \infty}\brac{ \lambda {\sum_{n=1}^N a_n} + \mu{\sum_{n=1}^N b_n}}\qquad \text{[linearity of finite sums]}\\ \amp = \lim_{N \to \infty}\brac{ \lambda\sum_{n=1}^N a_n} + \lim_{N \to \infty}\brac{\mu\sum_{n=1}^N b_n} \qquad \text{[addition rule]}\\ \amp = \lambda \lim_{N \to \infty}\sum_{n=1}^N a_n + \mu \lim_{N \to \infty}\sum_{n=1}^N b_n\qquad \text{[multiplication rule]}. \end{align*}

Lecture 4.6. Tests for convergence.

(a)

State and prove how absolute convergence implies convergence, together with the "infinite triangle inequality".
Solution.
Proposition 4.7. Absolute convergence implies convergence.

Let \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be a sequence of real numbers. If \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty |a_n| \lt \infty\) then the series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) converges to a finite real number.

In addition, we have the infinite triangle inequality

\begin{equation} \abs{\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n }\leq \sum_{n=1}^\infty |a_n|.\tag{4.1} \end{equation}
Proof.

Recall that the limit \(\sum_n |a_n|\) always exists (but is possibly infinite), by the monotone convergence principle (Proposition 3.3), as in Remark 4.3. To prove that the series \(\sum_n a_n\) converges, it suffices to prove that the sequence of partial sums \(\sum_{n=1}^N a_n\) is a Cauchy sequence (Definition 3.16), since Cauchy sequences converge (Proposition 3.17). To this end, let \(\eps \gt 0\text{.}\) Since we are assuming that \(\sum_{n=1}^N |a_n|\) tends to a real limit as \(N \to \infty\text{,}\) we know that this is a Cauchy sequence (Proposition 3.17). Hence there exists \(N_0 \in \N\) such that for all \(M\gt N \geq N_0\) we have

\begin{equation*} \eps \geq \abs{\sum_{n=1}^M |a_n| - \sum_{n=1}^N |a_n|} = \sum_{n=N+1}^M |a_n|. \end{equation*}

By the triangle inequality, for all \(M \gt N \geq N_0\) we have that

\begin{equation*} \abs{ \sum_{n=1}^M a_n - \sum_{n=1}^N a_n} = \abs{\sum_{n=N+1}^M a_n} \leq \sum_{n=N+1}^M |a_n| \leq \eps. \end{equation*}

This establishes the Cauchy property, as claimed.

To prove the infinite triangle inequality (4.1) we first observe the finite triangle inequality

\begin{equation*} -\sum_{n=1}^N |a_n| \leq \sum_{n=1}^N a_n \leq \sum_{n=1}^N |a_n| \end{equation*}

The infinite triangle inequality follows on letting \(N \to \infty\) and applying the inequality rule for limits (Proposition 2.2) to either side of the above.

(b)

State and prove the "\(n\)th term test" for non-convergence of infinite series.
Solution.
Proposition 4.8. \(n\)th term test.
Suppose that the sequence \((a_n)_n\) does not tend to zero, written \(a_n \not\to 0\) (or equivalently \(|a_n| \not\to 0\)). Then the series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) does not converge.
Proof.
We prove the contrapositive 1 . To this end, suppose that \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) converges to a real number. Then by Proposition 3.17, the sequence of partial sums
\begin{equation*} \brac{\sum_{n=1}^{N} a_n}_{N=1}^\infty \end{equation*}
is a Cauchy sequence. Hence for any \(\eps \gt 0\) there exists \(N_0 = N_0(\eps)\) such that for all \(N, M\geq N_0(\eps)\) we have
\begin{equation*} \abs{\sum_{n=1}^M a_n - \sum_{n=1}^N a_n} \leq \eps. \end{equation*}
In particular, if \(N-1 \geq N_0(\eps)\) then we have
\begin{equation*} \abs{\sum_{n=1}^N a_n - \sum_{n=1}^{N-1} a_n}\leq \eps. \end{equation*}
It follows that \(a_N \to 0\) according to Definition 1.11.

(c)

State and prove the "comparison test" for convergence of infinite series.
Solution.
Proposition 4.9. Comparison test.
Let \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) and \((b_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be sequences of real numbers. Suppose that \(|a_n| \leq b_n\) for all \(n\) and
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n \lt \infty. \end{equation*}
Then \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) converges absolutely.
Proof.
Notice that we must have \(b_n \geq 0\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Hence for any \(N\) we have
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^N |a_n| \leq \sum_{n=1}^N b_n \leq \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n. \end{equation*}
Therefore the sequence of partial sums \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^N |a_n|\) is an increasing sequence bounded above by the real number \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n\text{.}\) Hence, by the monotone convergence principle (Proposition 3.3), the sequence of partial sums \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^N |a_n|\) converges to a real number. This proves absolute convergence (and hence convergence).

(d)

State and prove the "limit comparison test" for convergence of infinite series.
Solution.
Corollary 4.10. Limit comparison test.
Let \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) and \((b_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be sequences of real numbers with \(b_n \gt 0\) positive for all \(n\text{.}\) Suppose that 2 
\begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to \infty}\frac{|a_n|}{b_n} \lt \infty \quad\text{and} \quad\sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n \lt \infty. \end{equation*}
Then \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) converges absolutely.
Proof.
Since the sequence \(\brac{a_n/b_n}_{n=1}^\infty\) is convergent, it is bounded (Proposition 1.15). Hence there exists \(M\gt 0\) such that for all \(n\) we have \(|a_n|/b_n \leq M \text{,}\) or equivalently
\begin{equation*} |a_n| \leq Mb_n\text{.} \end{equation*}
By linearity of series (Proposition 4.5) we have
\begin{equation*} \sum_n(Mb_n) = M\sum_n b_n \lt \infty\text{.} \end{equation*}
So the corollary follows from the comparison test (Proposition 4.9).

(e)

State and prove the "ratio test" for convergence of infinite series.
Solution.
Corollary 4.11. Ratio test.

Let \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be a sequence of positive real numbers.

If \(\displaystyle \lim_{n\to \infty}\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \lt 1 \) then \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \lt \infty.\)

If \(\displaystyle \lim_{n\to \infty}\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \gt 1 \) then \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n =\infty.\)

Proof.

Write

\begin{equation*} r:=\lim_{n\to \infty}\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}. \end{equation*}

Let us first suppose that \(r \lt 1\text{.}\) Let \(c\) be a real number satisfying \(r\lt c \lt 1\text{,}\) for instance \(c := (r+1)/2\text{.}\) Set

\begin{equation*} \eps := c - r \gt 0. \end{equation*}

Then there exists \(N\) such that for \(n \geq N\) we have

\begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}- r}\leq \eps, \end{equation*}

in particular

\begin{equation*} \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\leq r+ \eps = c. \end{equation*}

This implies that for \(n \gt N\) we have

\begin{equation*} a_{n} = a_{n-1} \frac{a_{n}}{a_{n-1}} \leq c a_{n-1}. \end{equation*}

Re-applying this argument, provided that \(n-1 \gt N\) we have

\begin{equation*} a_n \leq c^2 a_{n-2}. \end{equation*}

Iterating then gives that for \(n \gt N\) we have

\begin{equation*} a_n \leq c^{n-N} a_{N} \end{equation*}

Therefore, there exists a constant \(M\gt0\) such that for all \(n\) we have

\begin{equation*} a_n \leq Mc^n. \end{equation*}

For instance, one could take

\begin{equation*} M:=\max\set{a_1c^{-1},\dots, a_Nc^{-N}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(c \lt 1\text{,}\) we may sum the geometric series (Example 4.4) to deduce that \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty Mc^n \lt \infty\text{.}\) Hence the comparison test (Proposition 4.9) implies that \(\sum_n a_n \lt \infty\text{.}\)

Next suppose that the limit is \(\gt 1\text{.}\) Let \(C\) be a real number satisfying

\begin{equation*} 1 \lt C \lt \lim_{n\to \infty}\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}. \end{equation*}

Then a similar argument to that given above yields \(N \in \N\) and \(M\gt 0\) such that

\begin{equation*} \frac{C^n}{M} \leq a_n \quad \text{for all } n \geq N. \end{equation*}

Since \(C \gt 1\) the geometric series diverges \(\sum_{n=N}^\infty M^{-1}C^n = \infty\) (see Example 4.4), so the contrapositive of the comparison test (Proposition 4.9) implies that \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n = \infty\text{.}\)

(f)

State and prove the "alternating series test" for convergence of infinite series.
Solution.
Proposition 4.12. Alternating series test.
Let \((a_n)_n\) denote a decreasing sequence of non-negative reals, so that \(a_n \geq a_{n+1} \geq 0\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Suppose that \(a_n \to 0\text{.}\) Then the series
\begin{equation*} a_1-a_2+a_3-a_4 + \dots = \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1} a_n \end{equation*}
converges.
Before proving the alternating series test, we prove the following key inequality.
Lemma 4.13.
For any decreasing sequence \(b_1 \geq b_2 \geq \dots \geq b_n\geq 0\) of non-negative reals, with finitely many terms, we have the inequalities
\begin{equation} 0 \leq b_1 - b_2 + \dots + (-1)^{n+1} b_n \leq b_1.\tag{4.2} \end{equation}
Proof of Lemma 4.13.
We prove this by induction on \(n\text{,}\) the number of terms in the sequence. The basis case, when \(n=1\text{,}\) follows from the inequalities
\begin{equation*} 0 \leq b_1 \leq b_1. \end{equation*}
For the inductive step, we may suppose that
\begin{equation*} 0 \leq b_2 -b_3 + \dots + (-1)^n b_{n} \leq b_2. \end{equation*}
It follows that
\begin{equation*} b_1 - b_2 + \dots + (-1)^{n+1} b_n = b_1- \brac{b_2 -b_3+ \dots + (-1)^{n} b_n} \geq b_1-b_2 \geq 0 \end{equation*}
and that
\begin{equation*} b_1 - b_2 + \dots + (-1)^{n+1} b_n = b_1- \brac{b_2 -b_3+ \dots + (-1)^{n} b_n} \leq b_1- 0= b_1. \end{equation*}
Proof of alternating series test (Proposition 4.12).
Applying the inequality (4.2), for any \(N \gt M\) we have
\begin{align*} \abs{\sum_{n=1}^N(-1)^{n+1} a_n -\sum_{n=1}^M(-1)^{n+1} a_n } \amp= \abs{ \sum_{n=M+1}^N (-1)^{n+1} a_n}\\ \amp = a_{M+1} - a_{M+2} + \dots + (-1)^{N-M+1} a_N\\ \amp \leq a_{M+1}. \end{align*}
Since \(a_{n} \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\) there exists \(N_0 = N_0(\eps)\) such that for all \(n \geq N_0(\eps)\) we have \(a_{n} \leq \eps\text{.}\) In particular, if \(N \gt M \geq N_0(\eps)\) then
\begin{equation*} \abs{\sum_{n=1}^N(-1)^{n+1} a_n -\sum_{n=1}^M(-1)^{n+1} a_n } \leq a_{M+1} \leq \eps. \end{equation*}
It follows that the sequence of partial sums
\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^N (-1)^{n+1} a_n \end{equation*}
is a Cauchy sequence (Definition 3.16), hence convergent (see Proposition 3.17).
