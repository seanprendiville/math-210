    Subsection 10.1 Self-test questions for lecture 19

    - 10.1. Continuous functions are Riemann integrable.

    State and prove how a continuous function on a closed interval is Riemann integrable.
    Solution.
    Proposition 10.2. Continuous functions are Riemann integrable.
    Let \(a, b\in \R\) with \(a \lt b\text{.}\) Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be a continuous function. Then \(f\) is Riemann integrable on \([a,b]\text{.}\)
    Proof.
    Let us suppose that \([a,b] = [0, 1]\text{,}\) the general case being similar. Since \(f\) is a continuous function on a closed interval, it is uniformly continuous (Theorem 6.9). Hence by definition of uniform continuity (Definition 6.6):
    \begin{equation*} (\forall \eps \gt 0)(\exists \delta = \delta(\eps) \gt 0)(\forall x,y\in [a,b]) [|x-y| \leq \delta \implies |f(x)-f(y)| \leq \eps]. \end{equation*}
    Let
    \begin{equation*} \delta := \delta\brac{\eps} \qquad \text{and} \qquad N \geq \delta^{-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Then for any \(n = 1, \dots, N\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \sup_{[\frac{n-1}{N}, \frac{n}{N}]} f - \inf_{[\frac{n-1}{N}, \frac{n}{N}]} f \leq \eps. \end{equation*}
    Hence with respect to the subdivision \(0/N \lt 1/N \lt \dots \lt N/N\) we have
    \begin{align*} 0 \amp \leq U(f;[0,1])-L(f;[0,1])\\ \amp \leq U\brac{f;\tfrac{0}{N}, \tfrac{1}{N} , \dots , \tfrac{N}{N}} - L\brac{f;\tfrac{0}{N}, \tfrac{1}{N} , \dots , \tfrac{N}{N}} \leq \sum_{n=1}^N \tfrac{\eps}{N} = \eps. \end{align*}
    Letting \(\eps \to 0\text{,}\) it follows that \(U(f;[0,1]) = L(f;[0,1])\) by the inequality rule for function limits.
    Example 10.3.
    The function \(1_{\Q \cap [0,1]}\) is not Riemann integrable on \([0,1]\text{.}\)
    As in Example 5.26, the key observation in establishing our claim is to note that every real number \(x_0\) has a sequence of rationals \(q_n \in \Q\) such that \(q_n \to x_0\text{.}\) For instance, one may take
    \begin{equation*} q_n := \frac{\floor{nx_0}}{n} \in \left(x_0 -\frac{1}{n}, x_0\right]\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and employ the two-sided sandwich rule (Proposition 2.10).
    One consequence of this observation is that any non-empty open interval contains a rational number, since one may approximate the mid-point by a rational. It follows that if \(0 \leq x \lt y \leq 1\) then \((x,y)\) contains a rational \(q\in \Q\) and \((x+\sqrt{2},y+\sqrt{2})\) also contains a rational \(\tilde q \in \Q\text{.}\) Hence \((x,y)\) contains both a rational \(q\) and an irrational \(q-\sqrt{2}\text{.}\) It follows that
    \begin{equation*} \sup_{[x,y]} 1_{\Q\cap [0,1]} = 1 \quad \text{and}\quad \inf_{[x,y]} 1_{\Q\cap [0,1]} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Hence for any subdivision \(0 = x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = 1\) we have
    \begin{equation*} U(f;x_0,\dots, x_N) = \sum_{n=1}^N (x_n-x_{n-1}) \sup_{[x_{n-1}, x_n]} 1_{\Q\cap [0,1]} = \sum_{n=1}^N (x_n-x_{n-1}) = 1. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(U(f;[0,1]) = 1\text{.}\) Similarly
    \begin{equation*} L(f;[0,1]) = 0. \end{equation*}
    Therefore the function \(1_{\Q\cap [0,1]}\) is not Riemann integrable.

    - 10.4. Integral of derivative fundamental theorem of calculus.

    State and prove the "integral of derivative" version of the fundamental theorem of calculus.
    Solution.
    Theorem 10.5. Fundamental theorem of calculus - integral of derivative.
    Let \(f : (a,b) \to \R\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f\) is differentiable on \((a,b)\text{.}\) Given \(x,y \in (a,b)\) with \(x \lt y\text{,}\) suppose that \(f'\) is Riemann integrable on \([x,y]\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \int_x^y f' = f(y) - f(x). \end{equation*}
    Proof.
    Let \(x = x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = y\) denote a subdivision of \([x,y]\text{.}\) For each \(n\text{,}\) the mean value theorem (Theorem 8.7) gives \(y_n \in (x_{n-1}, x_n)\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f(x_n) - f(x_{n-1}) = f'(y_n)(x_n-x_{n-1}). \end{equation*}
    Hence
    \begin{equation*} f(y) - f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^N \sqbrac{f(x_n)-f(x_{n-1})} = \sum_{n=1}^N (x_n-x_{n-1})f'(y_n). \end{equation*}
    Thus
    \begin{equation*} L(f';x_0, \dots, x_N) \leq f(y)-f(x) \leq U(f';x_0, \dots, x_N). \end{equation*}
    Since this holds for all subdivisions \(x = x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = y\text{,}\) it follows from the definition of the upper and lower Riemann integrals (Definition 9.4) that
    \begin{equation*} L(f'; [a,b]) \leq f(y)-f(x) \leq U(f';[a,b]). \end{equation*}
    Since \(f'\) is Riemann integrable on \([x,y]\text{,}\) we have \(L(f';[x,y]) = U(f';[x,y]) = \int_x^y f'\text{.}\) We deduce that \(f(y)-f(x) = \int_x^y f'\text{.}\)

    - 10.6. Derivative of integral fundamental theorem of calculus.

    State and prove the "derivative of integral" version of the fundamental theorem of calculus.
    Solution.
    Theorem 10.7. Fundamental theorem of calculus - derivative of integral.
    Let \(a, b \in \R\) with \(a \lt b\text{.}\) Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be Riemann integrable. Define the function \(F : [a,b] \to \R\) at \(x \in [a,b]\) by
     1 
    Here we interpret \(\int_a^a f\) as 0.
    \begin{equation*} F(x) := \int_a^x f. \end{equation*}
    Then \(F\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) Moreover, if \(f\) is continuous at \(x_0 \in (a,b)\) then \(F\) is differentiable at \(x_0\) and we have
    \begin{equation*} F'(x_0) = f(x_0). \end{equation*}
    Proof.
    For \(x,y \in [a,b]\) with \(x \lt y\text{,}\) the interval additivity property (Proposition 9.16) gives that
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\int_a^x f - \int_a^{y} f} = \abs{\int_x^y f}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(f\) is Riemann integrable it is bounded, hence there exists \(M\in \R\) such that
    \begin{equation*} -M \leq \inf_{[a,b]}f \leq \sup_{[a,b]} f\leq M\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Hence trivial bounds for the integral (9.1) give that
    \begin{equation*} \abs{F(x)-F(y)} = \abs{\int_x^y f} \leq (y-x)M. \end{equation*}
    It follows that \(F\) is uniformly continuous on taking \(\delta(\eps):= \eps/M\) in the definition of uniform continuity (Definition 6.6).
    Next, suppose that \(f \) is continuous at \(x_0 \in (a,b)\text{.}\) Letting \(\eps \gt 0\text{,}\) there exists \(\delta = \delta(\eps) \gt 0\) such that if \(x \in [a,b]\) and \(|x-x_0| \leq \delta\) then \(|f(x)-f(x_0)| \leq \eps\text{.}\) It follows that if \(x\in [a,b]\) with \(x_0 \lt x\lt x_0+\delta\) then by linearity of integration (Theorem 9.13) we have
    \begin{align*} \abs{\frac{F(x) - F(x_0)}{x-x_0} - f(x_0)}\amp = \recip{x-x_0}\abs{\int_{x_0}^{x} \brac{f(t)-f(x_0)}\intd t }\\ \amp \leq \recip{x-x_0}\int_{x_0}^{x} \eps \intd t = \eps. \end{align*}
    A similar argument shows that if \(x\in [a,b]\) with \(x_0-\delta \lt x\lt x_0\) then
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{F(x) - F(x_0)}{x-x_0} - f(x_0)} = \recip{x_0-x}\abs{\int_{x}^{x_0} \brac{f(t)-f(x_0)}\intd t} \leq \eps. \end{equation*}
    Hence by the definition of differentiability (Definition 7.2) we have \(F'(x_0) = f(x_0)\text{.}\)

    Subsection 10.2 Self-test questions for lecture 20

    - 10.8. Uniform convergence.

    (a)
    Define what it means for a sequence of functions to converge pointwise.
    Solution.
    Definition 10.9. Pointwise convergence.
    For each \(n \in \N\) let \(f_n:S \to \R\) be a real-valued function with domain \(S\) (not necessarily a set of real numbers). Let \(f : S \to \R\text{.}\) We say that the sequence of functions \((f_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) converges pointwise to \(f\) on \(S\) if
    \begin{equation*} (\forall x \in S)\sqbrac{\lim_{n\to \infty} f_n(x) = f(x)}. \end{equation*}
    (b)
    Define what it means for a sequence of functions to converge uniformly.
    Solution.
    Definition 10.10. Uniform convergence.
    For each \(n \in \N\) let \(f_n:S \to \R\) be a real-valued function with domain \(S\) (not necessarily a set of real numbers). Let \(f : S \to \R\text{.}\) We say that the sequence of functions \((f_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) converges uniformly to \(f\) on \(S\) if
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to \infty} \sup_{S}| f-f_n| = 0. \end{equation*}
    (c)
    State and prove how uniform convergence implies pointwise convergence.
    Solution.
    Proposition 10.11. Uniform convergence implies pointwise convergence.
    Suppose that the \(f_n\) converge uniformly to \(f\) on \(S\text{.}\) Then the \(f_n\) converge pointwise to \(f\) on \(S\text{.}\)
    Proof.
    Fix \(x \in S\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \leq \sup_{S} |f_n-f| \to 0 \quad \text{as} \quad n \to \infty. \end{equation*}
    Hence by the sandwich rule (Proposition 3.6) we have \(f_n(x) \to f(x)\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\)
    (d)
    Give an example to show that pointwise convergence does not necessarily imply uniform convergence.
    Solution.
    Example 10.12. Pointwise convergence does not imply uniform convergence.
    Define \(f_n : (0, 1) \to \R\) by
    \begin{equation*} f_n(x) = 1_{[\frac{1}{n}, 1)}. \end{equation*}
    Then the \(f_n\) converge pointwise to \(1_{(0,1)}\text{,}\) but not uniformly.
    Proof.
    Fix \(x \in (0,1)\text{.}\) Setting \(N = N(x) := \ceil{1/x}\text{,}\) if \(n \geq N\) then \(1/n \leq x\) so
    \begin{equation*} f_n(x) = 1 = 1_{(0,1)}(x). \end{equation*}
    Thus \(f_n(x) \to 1_{(0,1)}(x)\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\) For any \(n \in \N\text{,}\) setting \(x_n := 1/(2n)\) gives \(f_n(x_n) = 0\) and \(1_{(0,1)}(x_n) = 1\text{.}\) Therefore, for any \(n \in \N\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \sup_{(0,1)} |f_n-1_{(0,1)}| = 1. \end{equation*}
    This supremum does not tend to 0 as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\)

    - 10.13. Riemann integrability is preserved under uniform convergence.

    State and prove how Riemann integrability is preserved under uniform convergence.
    Solution.
    Proposition 10.14. Integrability is preserved under uniform convergence.
    Let \(f, f_1, f_2, \dots:[a,b] \to \R\text{.}\) Suppose that the \(f_n\) converge uniformly to \(f\) on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Suppose that each \(f_n\) is Riemann integrable on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is Riemann integrable on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Furthermore
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f = \lim_{n\to \infty} \int_a^b f_n. \end{equation*}
    Proof.
    Let \(\eps \gt 0\text{.}\) By uniform convergence, there exists \(N \in \N\) such that for all \(n \geq N\) and all \(x \in [a,b]\) we have
    \begin{equation*} f_n(x)-\eps \leq f(x) \leq f_n(x) + \eps. \end{equation*}
    In particular, since \(f_n\) is bounded on \([a,b]\text{,}\) so is \(f\text{.}\) It follows that
    \begin{align*} \int_a^bf_n-\eps(b-a) = \int_a^b(f_n-\eps) \leq L(f;[a,b]) \leq U(f; \amp [a,b]) \leq \int_a^b(f_n+\eps)\\ \amp = \int_a^bf_n+\eps(b-a) . \end{align*}
    Hence by two applications of the sandwich rule (Proposition 2.10 and Proposition 2.10) we deduce that
    \begin{equation*} U(f;[a,b]) \leq \lim_{n \to \infty} \int_a^b f_n \leq L(f;[a,b]). \end{equation*}
    The claimed result follows.
    Example 10.15. Pointwise convergence does not imply convergence of integrals.
    Define \(f_n : [0, 1] \to \R\) by
    \begin{equation*} f_n(x) = n1_{(0,\frac{1}{n})}. \end{equation*}
    Then the \(f_n\) are Riemann integrable functions which converge pointwise to the zero function, yet
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to \infty}\int_0^1 f_n \neq \int_0^1\brac{\lim_{n\to\infty} f_n(x)} \intd x \end{equation*}
    Proof.
    Fix \(x \in (0,1]\text{.}\) Taking \(N=N(x) =1/x\text{,}\) if \(n \geq N\) then \(1/n \leq x\) so \(f_n(x) = 0 \text{.}\) Thus \(f_n(x) \to 0\) on \([0,1]\) (checking convergence at the endpoint \(x=0\) is trivial).
    By evaluating lower/upper Riemann sums, one can check that
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^1 f_n = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So it is not the case that
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^1 f_n \to \int_0^1 0\quad \text{ as } \quad n \to \infty\text{.} \end{equation*}

    - 10.16. Continuity is preserved under uniform convergence.

    State how continuity is preserved under uniform convergence (the proof is non-examinable).
    Solution.
    Proposition 10.17. Continuity is preserved under uniform convergence.
    Let \(f, f_1, f_2, \dots:D \to \R\) be real-valued functions on a real domain \(D \subset \R\text{.}\) Suppose that the \(f_n\) converge uniformly to \(f\) on \(D\text{.}\) Suppose that each \(f_n\) is continuous at \(x_0 \in D\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is continuous at \(x_0\text{.}\)
    Proof (non-examinable).
    To show that \(f\) is continuous at \(x_0\) it suffices to establish that
    \begin{equation*} (\forall \eps \gt 0)(\exists \delta= \delta(\eps) \gt 0)(\forall x \in D)[|x-x_0|\leq \delta \implies |f(x)-f(x_0)| \leq \eps]. \end{equation*}
    Since \(f_n\) converges to \(f\) uniformly, there exists \(n\) for which
    \begin{equation*} \sup_D |f_n-f| \leq \trecip{3} \eps. \end{equation*}
    Since \(f_n\) is continuous at \(x_0\text{,}\) there exists \(\delta \gt 0\) such that if \(x \in D\) satisfies \(|x-x_0| \leq \delta\) then \(|f_n(x)-f_n(x_0)| \leq \trecip{3}\eps\text{.}\) Hence
    \begin{align*} |f(x)-f(x_0)| \amp \leq |f(x)-f_n(x)| + |f_n(x)-f_n(x_0)| + |f_n(x_0) - f(x_0)|\\ \amp \leq \sup_D |f_n-f| + |f_n(x)-f_n(x_0)| + \sup_D |f_n-f|\\ \amp \leq \trecip{3} \eps +\trecip{3} \eps +\trecip{3} \eps = \eps. \end{align*}
