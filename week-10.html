Section 10 Week 10

Lecture 10.1. The fundamental theorem of calculus.

(a)

State and prove the "derivative of integral" version of the fundamental theorem of calculus.
Solution.
Theorem 10.2. Fundamental theorem of calculus - derivative of integral.
Let \(a, b \in \R\) with \(a \lt b\text{.}\) Let \(f : [a,b] \to \R\) be Riemann integrable. Define the function \(F : [a,b] \to \R\) at \(x \in [a,b]\) by 1 
\begin{equation*} F(x) := \int_a^x f. \end{equation*}
Then \(F\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) Moreover, if \(f\) is continuous at \(x_0 \in (a,b)\) then \(F\) is differentiable at \(x_0\) and we have
\begin{equation*} F'(x_0) = f(x_0). \end{equation*}
Proof.
For \(x,y \in [a,b]\) with \(x \lt y\text{,}\) the interval additivity property (Proposition 9.19) gives that
\begin{equation*} \abs{\int_a^x f - \int_a^{y} f} = \abs{\int_x^y f}. \end{equation*}
By the trivial upper bound for the integral ((8.6)) and the integral triangle inequality (Proposition 9.11) we have
\begin{equation*} \abs{\int_x^y f} \leq \int_x^y \abs{f} \leq (y-x)\sup_{[x,y]} |f|. \end{equation*}
It follows that for \(x, x_0 \in [a,b]\) we have
\begin{equation*} |F(x)-F(x_0)| \leq \abs{x-x_0} \sup_{[a,b]} |f|. \end{equation*}
Since \(\sup_{[a,b]} |f| \lt \infty\text{,}\) the sandwich rule (Proposition 2.6) gives that
\begin{equation*} |F(x)-F(x_0)| \to 0 \quad \text{as}\quad x \to x_0 \quad \text{through} \quad [a,b]. \end{equation*}
Thus \(F\) is continuous at \(x_0\text{.}\) Next, suppose that \(f \) is continuous at \(x_0 \in (a,b)\text{.}\) Letting \(\eps \gt 0\text{,}\) there exists \(\delta = \delta(\eps) \gt 0\) such that if \(x \in [a,b]\) and \(|x-x_0| \leq \delta\) then \(|f(x)-f(x_0)| \leq \eps\text{.}\) Notice that for \(h \gt0\) we have
\begin{equation*} h^{-1} \int_{x_0}^{x_0+h} f(x_0) \intd x = f(x_0)h^{-1} \int_{x_0}^{x_0+h} 1 = f(x_0). \end{equation*}
It follows that if \(0 \lt h \leq \delta\) then by linearity of integration (Theorem 9.6) and the integral triangle inequality (Proposition 9.11) we have
\begin{align*} \abs{\frac{\int_a^{x_0+h} f - \int_a^{x_0}f}{h} - f(x_0)}\amp \leq h^{-1}\int_{x_0}^{x_0+h} \abs{f(x)-f(x_0)}\intd x \\ \amp \leq h^{-1}\int_{x_0}^{x_0+h}\eps = \eps. \end{align*}
Hence for \(0 \lt h \leq \delta\) we have
\begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{F(x_0 + h)-F(x_0)}{h} - f(x_0)} \leq \eps. \end{equation*}
A similar argument proves the same inequality when \(-\delta \lt h \lt 0\text{.}\) It follows from the definition of differentiability (Definition 6.22) that \(F'(x_0) = f(x_0)\text{.}\)

(b)

State and prove the "integral of derivative" version of the fundamental theorem of calculus.
Solution.
Theorem 10.3. Fundamental theorem of calculus - integral of derivative.
Let \(f : (a,b) \to \R\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f\) is differentiable on \((a,b)\text{.}\) Given \(x,y \in (a,b)\) with \(x \lt y\text{,}\) suppose that \(f'\) is Riemann integrable on \([x,y]\text{.}\) Then
\begin{equation*} \int_x^y f' = f(y) - f(x). \end{equation*}
Proof.
Let \(x = x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = y\) denote a subdivision of \([x,y]\text{.}\) For each \(n\text{,}\) the mean value theorem (Theorem 7.10) gives \(y_n \in (x_{n-1}, x_n)\) such that
\begin{equation*} f(x_n) - f(x_{n-1}) = f'(y_n)(x_n-x_{n-1}). \end{equation*}
Hence
\begin{equation*} f(y) - f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^N \sqbrac{f(x_n)-f(x_{n-1})} = \sum_{n=1}^N (x_n-x_{n-1})f'(y_n). \end{equation*}
Thus
\begin{equation*} L(f';x_0, \dots, x_N) \leq f(y)-f(x) \leq U(f';x_0, \dots, x_N). \end{equation*}
Since this holds for all subdivisions \(x = x_0 \lt \dots \lt x_N = y\text{,}\) it follows from the definition of the upper and lower Riemann integrals (Definition 8.17) that
\begin{equation*} L(f'; [a,b]) \leq f(y)-f(x) \leq U(f';[a,b]). \end{equation*}
Since \(f'\) is Riemann integrable on \([x,y]\text{,}\) we have \(L(f';[x,y]) = U(f';[x,y]) = \int_x^y f'\text{.}\) We deduce that \(f(y)-f(x) = \int_x^y f'\text{.}\)

(c)

Using the fundamental theorem of calculus, state and prove the integration by parts formula.
Solution.
Corollary 10.4. Integration by parts.
Let \(f ,g: (a,b) \to \R\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable on \((a,b)\text{.}\) Given \(x,y \in (a,b)\) with \(x \lt y\text{,}\) suppose that both \(f'\) and \(g'\) are Riemann integrable on \([x,y]\text{.}\) Then
\begin{equation*} \int_x^y f'g = f(y)g(y) - f(x)g(x) - \int_x^y fg'. \end{equation*}
Proof.
By the product rule for differentiation (Proposition 7.2) we have \((fg)' = f'g + fg'\text{.}\) Both \(f'g\) and \(fg'\) are integrable on \([x,y]\text{,}\) as they are products of Riemann integrable functions (Theorem 9.13). Therefore \((fg)'\) is also integrable on \([x,y]\text{,}\) by linearity of integration (Theorem 9.6). Hence by the fundamental theorem of calculus (Theorem 10.3), we have
\begin{equation*} \int_x^y \brac{f'g + fg'} = \int_x^y \brac{fg}' = f(y)g(y) - f(x)g(x). \end{equation*}
The result now follows from linearity of integration (Theorem 9.6).

(d)

Using the fundamental theorem of calculus, state and prove the change of variables formula, also known as integration by substitution.
Solution.
Corollary 10.5.
Change of variables formula 2 Let \(\phi: (a,b) \to (c,d)\) be a differentiable function with continuous derivative \(\phi'\text{.}\) Let \(f : (c,d) \to \R\) be continuous. Given \(x,y \in (a,b)\) we have 3 
\begin{equation*} \int_{\phi(x)}^{\phi(y)} f = \int_x^y \brac{f\circ \phi} \phi'. \end{equation*}
Proof.
The function \(f\circ \phi\) is integrable on any closed interval in \((a,b)\text{,}\) since the composition of continuous functions is continuous (Proposition 6.7). Hence the function \((f\circ \phi)\phi'\) is integrable on any closed interval in \((a,b)\text{,}\) as it is the product of Riemann integrable functions (Theorem 9.13). Define the functions
\begin{equation*} F(s):=\int_{\phi(x)}^{s} f \quad\text{and}\quad G(t) :=\int_x^t \brac{f\circ \phi}\phi'. \end{equation*}
We would like to show that \(F(\phi(y)) = G(y)\text{.}\) By the fundamental theorem of calculus (Theorem 10.2) we have
\begin{equation*} F'(s) = f(s) \quad \text{and} \quad G'(t) = f(\phi(t))\phi'(t). \end{equation*}
By the chain rule (Theorem 7.3) we have
\begin{equation*} (F\circ\phi)'(t) = f(\phi(t))\phi'(t). \end{equation*}
Since functions on an interval with zero derivative are constant (Corollary 7.11) there exists \(C \in \R\) such that for all \(t\in (a,b)\) we have
\begin{equation*} (F\circ\phi)(t)-G(t) = C. \end{equation*}
Since \((F\circ \phi) (x) = 0\) and \(G(x) = 0\text{,}\) we deduce that \(C = 0\text{.}\) The required identity follows.

Lecture 10.6. Uniform convergence.

(a)

Define what it means for a sequence of functions to converge pointwise.
Solution.
Definition 10.7. Pointwise convergence.
For each \(n \in \N\) let \(f_n:S \to \R\) be a real-valued function with domain \(S\) (not necessarily a set of real numbers). Let \(f : S \to \R\text{.}\) We say that the sequence of functions \((f_n)_{n=1}^\infty\)converges pointwise to \(f\) on \(S\) if
\begin{equation*} (\forall x \in S)\sqbrac{\lim_{n\to \infty} f_n(x) = f(x)}. \end{equation*}

(b)

Define what it means for a sequence of functions to converge uniformly.
Solution.
Definition 10.8. Uniform convergence.
For each \(n \in \N\) let \(f_n:S \to \R\) be a real-valued function with domain \(S\) (not necessarily a set of real numbers). Let \(f : S \to \R\text{.}\) We say that the sequence of functions \((f_n)_{n=1}^\infty\)converges uniformly to \(f\) on \(S\) if
\begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to \infty} \sup_{S}| f-f_n| = 0. \end{equation*}

(c)

State and prove how uniform convergence implies pointwise convergence.
Solution.
Proposition 10.9. Uniform convergence implies pointwise convergence.
Suppose that the \(f_n\) converge uniformly to \(f\) on \(S\text{.}\) Then the \(f_n\) converge pointwise to \(f\) on \(S\text{.}\)
Proof.
Fix \(x \in S\text{.}\) Then
\begin{equation*} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \leq \sup_{S} |f_n-f| \to 0 \quad \text{as} \quad n \to \infty. \end{equation*}
Hence by the sandwich rule (Proposition 2.5) we have \(f_n(x) \to f(x)\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\)

(d)

Give an example to show that pointwise convergence does not necessarily imply uniform convergence.
Solution.
Example 10.10. Pointwise convergence does not imply uniform convergence.
Define \(f_n : (0, 1) \to \R\) by
\begin{equation*} f_n(x) = 1_{[\frac{1}{n}, 1)}. \end{equation*}
Then the \(f_n\) converge pointwise to \(1_{(0,1)}\text{,}\) but not uniformly.
Proof.
Fix \(x \in (0,1)\text{.}\) Setting \(N = N(x) := \ceil{1/x}\text{,}\) if \(n \geq N\) then \(1/n \leq x\) so
\begin{equation*} f_n(x) = 1 = 1_{(0,1)}(x). \end{equation*}
Thus \(f_n(x) \to 1_{(0,1)}(x)\) as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\) For any \(n \in \N\text{,}\) setting \(x_n := 1/(2n)\) gives \(f_n(x_n) = 0\) and \(1_{(0,1)}(x_n) = 1\text{.}\) Therefore, for any \(n \in \N\) we have
\begin{equation*} \sup_{(0,1)} |f_n-1_{(0,1)}| = 1. \end{equation*}
This supremum does not tend to 0 as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\)

(e)

State and prove how continuity is preserved under uniform convergence.
Solution.
Proposition 10.11. Continuity is preserved under uniform convergence.
Let \(f, f_1, f_2, \dots:D \to \R\) be real-valued functions on a real domain \(D \subset \R\text{.}\) Suppose that the \(f_n\) converge uniformly to \(f\) on \(D\text{.}\) Suppose that each \(f_n\) is continuous at \(x_0 \in D\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is continuous at \(x_0\text{.}\)
Proof.
To show that \(f\) is continuous at \(x_0\) it suffices to establish that
\begin{equation*} (\forall \eps \gt 0)(\exists \delta= \delta(\eps) \gt 0)(\forall x \in D)[|x-x_0|\leq \delta \implies |f(x)-f(x_0)| \leq \eps]. \end{equation*}
Since \(f_n\) converges to \(f\) uniformly, there exists \(n\) for which
\begin{equation*} \sup_D |f_n-f| \leq \trecip{3} \eps. \end{equation*}
Since \(f_n\) is continuous at \(x_0\text{,}\) there exists \(\delta \gt 0\) such that if \(x \in D\) satisfies \(|x-x_0| \leq \delta\) then \(|f_n(x)-f_n(x_0)| \leq \trecip{3}\eps\text{.}\) Hence
\begin{align*} |f(x)-f(x_0)| \amp \leq |f(x)-f_n(x)| + |f_n(x)-f_n(x_0)| + |f_n(x_0) - f(x_0)|\\ \amp \leq \sup_D |f_n-f| + |f_n(x)-f_n(x_0)| + \sup_D |f_n-f|\\ \amp \leq \trecip{3} \eps +\trecip{3} \eps +\trecip{3} \eps = \eps. \end{align*}

(f)

State and prove how Riemann integrability is preserved under uniform convergence.
Solution.
Proposition 10.12. Integrability is preserved under uniform convergence.
Let \(f, f_1, f_2, \dots:[a,b] \to \R\text{.}\) Suppose that the \(f_n\) converge uniformly to \(f\) on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Suppose that each \(f_n\) is Riemann integrable on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is Riemann integrable on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Furthermore
\begin{equation*} \int_a^b f = \lim_{n\to \infty} \int_a^b f_n. \end{equation*}
Proof.
Let \(\eps \gt 0\text{.}\) By uniform convergence, there exists \(N \in \N\) such that for all \(n \geq N\) and all \(x \in [a,b]\) we have
\begin{equation*} f_n(x)-\eps \leq f(x) \leq f_n(x) + \eps. \end{equation*}
In particular, since \(f_n\) is bounded on \([a,b]\text{,}\) so is \(f\text{.}\) It follows that
\begin{align*} \int_a^bf_n-\eps(b-a) = \int_a^b(f_n-\eps) \leq L(f;[a,b]) \leq U(f; \amp [a,b]) \leq \int_a^b(f_n+\eps)\\ \amp = \int_a^bf_n+\eps(b-a) . \end{align*}
Hence by two applications of the sandwich rule (Proposition 2.6 and Proposition 2.6) we deduce that
\begin{equation*} U(f;[a,b]) \leq \lim_{n \to \infty} \int_a^b f_n \leq L(f;[a,b]). \end{equation*}
The claimed result follows.

(g)

Give an example of a sequence of Riemann integrable functions which converge pointwise, but for which the limit is not Riemann integrable.
Solution.
Example 10.13.
Let \(q_1, q_2, \dots\) be an enumeration 4  of the rationals in \([0,1]\text{.}\) The sequence of functions \(f_n := 1_{\set{q_1, \dots, q_n}} : [0,1] \to \R\) converges pointwise to \(f := 1_{\Q \cap [0,1]}\) on \([0,1]\text{.}\) Each \(f_n\) is Riemann integrable on \([0,1]\) by Example 9.16 and linearity of the integral (Theorem 9.6). Moreover \(\int_0^1 f_n = 0\text{.}\) Yet by Proposition 9.24, the function \(f= 1_{\Q \cap [0,1]}\) is not Riemann integrable on \([0,1]\text{.}\) This example shows that, unlike uniform convergence, pointwise convergence is not sufficient to ensure Riemann integrability of the limit.
Proof.
It suffices to show that the \(f_n\) converge pointwise to \(f\) on \([0,1]\text{.}\) Let \(x \in [0,1]\text{.}\) If \(x \notin \Q\) then \(f_n(x) = 0\) for all \(n\) and \(f(x) = 0\text{.}\) Hence \(f_n(x) \to f(x)\) as \(n \to \infty\) when \(x \notin \Q\text{.}\) Next suppose that \(x \in \Q\text{.}\) Then \(x = q_N\) for some \(N \in \N\text{.}\) Hence \(f_n(x) = 1\) for all \(n \geq N\text{.}\) Also \(f(x) = 1\text{.}\) Therefore \(f_n(x) \to f(x)\) as \(n \to \infty\) when \(x \in \Q\text{.}\)
Here we interpret \(\int_a^a f\) as 0.
